Soft Climate

[![Soft Climate header](media/soft-climate-header.png)](media/soft-climate-header.png)

One day became several.

The next afternoon Ava came back from outside with Lia’s charger, a clean sweater, her book from the bedside table, and the ibuprofen from her bathroom cabinet. Lia stared at the things laid out on the chair and tried to remember whether she had clearly asked for any of them.

“I thought you might want your own things without having to go back yet,” Ava said.

It was such a clean sentence that Lia heard herself thanking her before she had decided whether to.

By the third day the hours had started to smooth out. Ava was always half a step ahead: soup before hunger turned mean, the curtains drawn before dusk could turn the windows into mirrors, her phone turned facedown with a quiet “You can answer tomorrow” when another message lit the screen. Marco once. Marin with a plain worried check-in. A missed call from the building. Nothing urgent, Ava kept saying, and Lia kept letting tomorrow take it.

By then she had stopped reaching for the kettle herself.

Not because she couldn’t. Because Ava always seemed to be crossing the room with a mug just before thirst turned conscious. The same with everything else. A square of dark chocolate after Lia forced down half a bowl of soup. A blanket over her legs while she was still only beginning to notice the chill. Touch that never lingered too long, but always arrived at the exact second the room threatened to tilt.

It should have felt uncanny. Mostly it felt good.

On the fourth evening they sat on opposite ends of the couch while an old black-and-white film flickered with the sound off. Rain trailed down the windows in silver threads. Ava had turned the captions on and the city down.

Lia was halfway through a bowl of ice cream she hadn’t wanted, until Ava put it in her hand. She stared at it for a moment, then said, “I can’t tell if I’m getting better or just getting used to you doing everything.”

Ava took the bowl before it could slide off Lia’s lap and set it on the table. “Those might not be different yet.”

“That’s not comforting.”

“It’s honest.” Ava looked at her. “You went from bracing all the time to not having to. Your body’s going to like that.”

Lia watched their reflections blur in the dark glass. “I don’t like how much I need this.”

“Maybe you just like not having to hold yourself together alone for once.”

The line landed because it was too close to true.

Ava reached over and tucked a loose strand of hair behind Lia’s ear. The touch was brief enough to deny itself if challenged. It stayed anyway.

“You wanted to live a little,” she said. “That wasn’t the mistake.”

Lia looked at her. “You make it sound simple.”

“I’m not saying it was simple.” Ava leaned back again, making space right after closing it. “I’m saying wanting out of the box didn’t mean you deserved what happened.”

The next day, when Ava stepped out for groceries, the quiet changed texture as soon as the door shut. Lia told herself it was only an hour, that adults did not go soft because one woman with good timing had gone downstairs and out into the city. Still, she found herself listening for the key in the lock.

That afternoon she tried to go home.

Her coat was on. Her hand was on the latch. The hallway outside smelled faintly of ozone and somebody else’s takeout. Normal building smells. Still, the idea of stepping into the elevator alone made something cold move through her ribs.

Ava came up behind her, not touching. “You can go,” she said.

Lia kept looking at the door.

“Or you can wait until it feels less like a dare.”

Lia’s fingers loosened on the latch before she meant them to. Her hand hurt from how hard she had been gripping it.

“That’s not fair,” she said.

“Maybe,” Ava said.

Lia pulled the coat off and left it over the back of a chair. Later, trying to think straight about that moment, she could never separate the unease from the relief. They had arrived braided.

That night she woke from a dream that wasn’t even the bar, not exactly; just a corridor, a locked throat, footsteps behind her moving at the same pace no matter how fast she went. She came up hard, breath shredding, the guest room dark except for a seam of hallway light under the door.

Before she could decide whether to be ashamed, Ava was there.

No rush. No questions. She sat on the edge of the bed and put her hand between Lia’s shoulder blades. Warm. Present. Impossible not to lean toward.

“Sorry,” Lia said into the dark.

“For what?”

“This.”

Ava’s hand moved once, slow and steady. “You’re not making a mess.”

She stayed until Lia’s breathing evened out, then left before the comfort could turn theatrical. In the morning there was coffee waiting and a small paper bag from the bakery downstairs. Inside were the almond pastries Lia liked but almost never bought for herself because they cost too much for something so temporary.

“You went out early,” Lia said.

Ava shrugged. “You slept.”

Two days later Ava stepped out again, longer this time. Tea on the table. Soup in the fridge. The lamp in the living room turned low before she left. The apartment was the same after the door shut. Same cedar smell. Same folded blanket on the couch. Same quiet. None of it sat the same without Ava moving through it.

Lia drifted from the kitchen to the window and back again, her phone lighting once on the counter with a message she didn’t open. The rooms felt larger than they had in days. Flatter. She stood in the middle of the living room and waited without wanting to call it waiting.

When the key finally turned in the lock, relief hit before recognition. Her shoulders dropped. The rooms stopped feeling so large.

That was the part she couldn’t quite look at straight: how quickly her body settled at the sound of Ava coming back.
