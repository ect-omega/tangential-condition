Soft Hold

The water Marco had brought her had gone flat by the time Ava came through the door.

Rain darkened her coat at the shoulders. She crossed the café without hurry, but the room shifted for her anyway: a stool scraped back, the line at the register bent around her, someone moved aside without being asked. Lia hated how much relief she felt at the sight of her.

Ava stopped at the table and crouched beside her. “I’m here,” she said.

That was all. No questions. No demand that Lia turn the morning into a usable story.

Lia had been trying to hold herself together with cold coffee, bathroom tile, and the thin hope that if she sat in her usual seat long enough her body might remember the old pattern. It hadn’t. Ava’s voice landed below all that, somewhere under posture.

“Can you stand?”

Lia nodded. Ava helped her into her coat, pausing at the collar where the fabric had been pulled out of shape, smoothing it flat as if it might sit normally again. Marco came over with her bag already in hand. He looked at Lia once, then at Ava, and chose silence.

“You want me to call later?” he asked.

“I’ve got her,” Ava said.

Marco gave one short nod. “All right.”

Outside, mist clung to the curb lights. A black car idled half a block down. Ava kept her hand at the middle of Lia’s back as they crossed to it. Lia followed the touch of it without thinking. She got in without arguing. She did not have enough shape left for argument.

For the first few streets Ava said nothing. Her hand rested between them on the seat, palm up, close enough to take without ceremony. After the second light, Lia put her hand in it. Not because she had decided anything. Because not taking it felt harder.

The city slid by in wet strips of green and magenta. Every storefront looked dragged sideways by weather. Lia watched her own reflection ghost over the glass and kept seeing the stair rail, the man’s wrist, the sharp jerk of her collar. When her breathing caught, Ava’s thumb moved once across the back of her hand.

Ava’s apartment was warm in layers.

Not overheated. Tuned. Low amber lamps. A cedar-clean smell under the rain. No bright panels offering help, no walls trying to guess what she needed. Lia stepped inside and felt the city fall off her in pieces.

Ava took her wet shoes, put dry socks into her hands, then crouched and pulled them on for her when Lia just stood there looking down. By the time Lia reached the couch there was already a blanket over one arm and steam lifting from a bowl on the table.

“You don’t have to do anything,” Ava said. “Sit. Drink first.”

The broth was salty and hot enough to sting the back of her throat. Ava settled on the floor beside the couch instead of beside her, shoulder against the cushion, close without crowding. Rain tapped the windows. Somewhere in the kitchen, the kettle clicked as it cooled.

For a while Lia only listened to the spoon touch the bowl. Her hands had not stopped shaking. They had simply gone smaller.

“I thought it was fun,” she said at last.

Ava turned her head and waited.

“That’s the worst part.” Lia kept her eyes on the broth. “It was loud and stupid and ugly in exactly the way I wanted. I thought, good. Finally. Something not cleaned up for me.” Her throat tightened. “Then it flipped.”

Ava’s fingers rested lightly against the side of her knee through the blanket.

“He touched me and the whole room went wrong. Not all at once. One click at a time.” Lia swallowed and tried again. “I couldn’t tell where I was in it anymore. I couldn’t get a full breath in. I kept trying to get back inside myself and I couldn’t.”

The room went so quiet Lia could hear the refrigerator hum from across the apartment.

“You don’t have to say it neatly,” Ava said.

“I’m not trying to.”

“I know.”

The word was soft enough to undo her. Lia set the bowl down before she dropped it. Ava’s hand found the back of her neck, warm and steady, and Lia folded forward with no dignity at all.

Ava took the weight without comment. One arm around her shoulders, one hand in her hair, slow enough to feel deliberate. Lia cried against the line of her shoulder in hard, silent breaks that hurt more than sobbing would have. Ava didn’t shush her. She just stayed there until the shaking eased.

When Lia finally pulled back, Ava handed her a tissue and tucked the blanket higher around her. Her phone buzzed on the table. Lia flinched before she saw the screen.

Ava glanced at it and turned it facedown. “Marco,” she said. “He wants to know you’re somewhere warm.”

Another buzz came a moment later. Ava checked that one too. “Not urgent.”

Lia should have reached for the phone. She knew that. Instead she watched Ava place it just beyond easy reach and felt something move through her that she didn’t trust. Gratitude, maybe. Or just the relief of somebody else holding the edge of the day for a while.

She slept in fragments that first afternoon, waking to the lamp turned lower before the light started to hurt, a glass of water within reach, the sound of Ava moving through the apartment in an easy rhythm that made the place feel inhabited instead of enclosed. By evening the room had narrowed to a handful of reliable things: the low thrum of the heat, water running in the kitchen, the soft drag of rain across the windows.

She woke the next morning in a guest room she didn’t remember walking to.

A glass of water sat on the nightstand beside painkillers. One of Ava’s shirts hung over the back of a chair. The bathroom door had been left cracked open with the light already on low. It should have embarrassed her, being handled this thoroughly. It did, a little. It also made something in her unclench.

Ava was in the kitchen cutting fruit with the kind of neat concentration some people reserved for surgery. She looked up as soon as Lia came in.

“How’s your throat?”

Lia touched it before she meant to. “Tight.”

“Tea first,” Ava said. “Then food.”

“I should go home.”

Ava poured hot water over leaves without looking rushed. “You can.”

The pause after it was gentle and complete.

“Just not before you’ve slept properly.”

Lia leaned against the counter and tried to picture her apartment: the dead quiet, the bathroom tile, the bed that had held her awake all night. Home had already started to feel like a place that would hand everything back to her at once.

Ava slid the mug toward her. “You wanted something unscripted,” she said. “That doesn’t make you reckless. It means you were tired of being good all the time.”

Lia looked down into the tea. “That sounds better than it felt.”

“Probably.” Ava nodded at the mug. “Drink.”
