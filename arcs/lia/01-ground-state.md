Ground State

[![Ground State header](media/ground-state-header.png)](media/ground-state-header.png)

The café window held the city in smeared bands of green and magenta, rain dragging neon sideways until every tower looked unstable. Inside, everything kept its little promises: grinders hissed, cups stacked true, receipts spat from the register in warm white curls. Lia liked that part. Machines that did what they said.

Marco set a saucer on the little round table by the window and grinned at her through the steam. “You know half the regulars time their mornings by you, right? Same seat, same real face. You make the place feel honest. No overlays, no drift. Just Lia.”

He meant it as affection. That was the problem. People always did.

Stable, unaugmented, reliable. The grounded one. She had heard versions of it so often the words no longer felt like compliments so much as municipal signage, clean arrows bolted above a door she had not realized was locking behind her. Even Marco, who loved people in a loose practical way, used her as evidence. See? Someone can stay real in this city and not dissolve.

The café was one of the few places where that version of her had fully taken root. Marco knew her order before she spoke. Ava knew to check here if Lia went silent. The morning crowd read her presence the way people read weather: a small confirmation that some patterns still held. She had become part of the room’s routine without ever consenting to be.

The city, meanwhile, never stopped asking. Transit glass outside offered passersby route smoothing and mood calibration in pale little blooms. A table ad across the room suggested improved hydration cadence to a woman wearing three soft-lens overlays at once. Even the building lights learned faces now, nudging warmer when they read stress, cooler when they read fatigue. No force. No hard sell. Just a thousand polite hands on the wheel.

Lia ignored it all on principle, which had once felt like spine. Lately it felt closer to reflex. That bothered her in ways she did not like to name. If every system in the city says yes and your whole identity is built on saying no, where exactly is the freedom in that? Counterprogramming was still programming. Different logo, same architecture.

Later, cutting through the pocket park behind the tram stop, she nearly collided with Marin coming around a rain-black plane tree. The park was barely a park, just a strip of wet benches and municipal shrubs boxed in by traffic, but it smelled briefly of soil instead of coolant, which was reason enough for people to pretend it was nature.

Marin had a folded umbrella under one arm and a takeout carton balanced in one hand. One lens shimmered faintly over her right eye before dimming when she recognized Lia. “There you are,” she said, smiling like she had found something she trusted. “I was just thinking about you.”

“That sounds ominous.”

“It isn’t. I was in a meeting where everyone kept talking like upgrades are basically weather now.” Marin shifted the carton to her other hand. “And I thought, no, there’s still Lia. Still no layers, no leasing out half your nervous system. Weirdly reassuring.”

Reassuring. Another soft nail.

Lia glanced at the runoff threading along the curb, carrying bits of leaf and foil toward the storm drains. “Happy to be useful as a moral exhibit.”

Marin laughed, missing the edge. “See? Even that. You don’t drift.” She tipped her head, taking Lia in with the casual certainty of someone naming a fact. “It’s good. You always look like yourself.”

Always look like yourself. As if that were simple. As if remaining legible in this city did not require a private kind of strain.

Marin checked the time on the slim band at her wrist and winced. “I’m late. But seriously. Don’t let anybody convert you.”

Then she was gone again, shoes clipping across the wet path, umbrella opening at the gate in a black clean bloom. The encounter lasted maybe a minute. It stayed longer than that.

Lia caught her reflection in the dark side window of a closed kiosk at the park edge: plain coat, hair tied back, face unlit by any active layer. Clean lines. Good posture. The whole golden-girl package, minus the gold. People saw discipline and called it peace. They saw refusal and called it strength. Nobody seemed interested in the labor cost.

That night Ava asked her to come upstairs after Marco killed the front sign and the last of the crowd bled into the street.

At the top of the stairwell, Ava caught lightly at Lia’s sleeve before she could step into the worst of the wind. “This side,” she said. “Unless you’re trying to be dramatic.”

“Only in very controlled doses.”

Ava smiled at that, small and private, and guided her a step closer to the wash of warmth coming off the HVAC units. Her fingers left no pressure, just heat through damp fabric, but Lia felt the place they’d been anyway.

The rooftop was slick with recent rain, planter boxes breathing out the mineral smell of wet soil while the vents pushed warm air into the cold. The city opened around them in stacked phosphor, all those tower skins flickering with ad-weather and transit telemetry. Far below, traffic moved in obedient ribbons. Above it, a drone passed like a red eyelid.

Ava leaned against the rail with her coat unbuttoned, as if temperature was a rumor other people dealt with. “You’ve been grinding your teeth all week,” she said, not looking at the skyline so much as at Lia reflected in the roofline glass. “I can hear it from across a room.”

Lia almost laughed. “That feels medically impossible.”

“Not medically. Socially.” Ava turned then, softer than Lia expected. She was standing close enough that Lia could smell rain in her hair and the faint bitter trace of coffee from downstairs. “You look tired.”

There were people who said that as an accusation, and people who said it like they’d found damage under the paint. Ava was the second kind. Worse, somehow.

“I’m fine,” Lia said.

Ava made a small face. Not disbelief. More like sadness at a boring pattern repeating itself. “That word does too much work for you.”

The wind shifted. Somewhere down the block a siren started up, got swallowed by the grid, then came back warped through distance. Ava moved an inch closer without making a production of it, shoulder nearly aligned with Lia’s. Lia could have stepped away. She didn’t.

“One of the things I like about you,” Ava said, “is that you don’t let the city auto-complete you. Most people lease out their edges and call it convenience.” She rested her forearms on the rail, eyes on the skyline, though every few seconds they came back to Lia. “But I think everyone around you hears that as permission to keep you fixed.”

Lia said nothing.

She knew, before Ava said the name, what direction this was going to bend. Virtualia sat between them these days like a third person with very good manners.

“I’m not trying to recruit you,” Ava said. “I know that’s what you think whenever I bring it up.”

“You bring it up a lot.”

Ava’s mouth tilted. “Maybe I look for reasons to keep you up here.”

Lia let out a breath that could almost pass for a laugh. “That sounds efficient.”

“Not everything is optimization.”

The line should have been light. It wasn’t. Ava’s voice had gone quieter, threaded with something more selective than casual concern. Lia looked out at the city instead of at her.

“Because you keep talking about it like it’s surrender,” Ava went on. “It isn’t. Not inherently. It can be expansion. More room, not less.”

A tower face a few blocks east was running one of those slow wellness ads with people smiling inside impossible clean light, their nervous systems apparently solved. “That’s how they sell it.”

“I’m not they.”

That landed harder than it should have. Maybe because it was true. Maybe because Ava said it like a correction meant only for Lia.

Ava pushed a wet strand of hair behind one ear. “You treat unchanged like it’s the same thing as free. It isn’t. Sometimes it’s just another lock. Better branding.”

The HVAC hummed. A plastic prayer flag someone had zip-tied to a planter snapped once in the wind, then went still. Ava turned a little more toward her now, forearm brushing the sleeve of Lia’s coat for an instant and not moving away quickly enough to feel accidental.

“Virtualia doesn’t have to replace you,” Ava said. “It doesn’t have to write over anything. Think of it as aperture. Additional bandwidth. A wider room for the same mind.”

There were cruder versions of that argument, all sales gloss and transcendence jargon. Lia knew how to swat those away. Ava’s version was worse because it held no sermon in it, no corporate perfume. Just warmth. Just care. Just the possibility that Lia’s resistance was not purity but fear that had learned to dress well.

“I’m not scared of more room,” Lia said.

Ava’s eyes flicked to her and stayed there. “No,” she said gently. “You’re scared of needing it.”

It was one of those sentences that did not sound loud until it hit. Lia felt it under the sternum, exact as a knuckle on glass.

For a second the wind died. The city noise below seemed to flatten and recede. Ava was close enough that turning her head would have changed the night by a degree neither of them could pretend not to notice. Lia felt the pull of that like heat against skin. She looked back out at the skyline instead.

Ava did not press. That was part of her. She never lunged at a boundary; she just kept finding ways to stand near it, patient as weather.

They stayed there a while after that, shoulder to shoulder with only the thinnest gap between them, watching the city self-correct. The cloud layer adjusted its brightness by a degree too subtle to name. A logistics drone rerouted around weather before the weather fully formed. Somewhere in the towers a hundred thousand people accepted a hundred thousand nudges and called the day efficient.

On the walk home Lia kept her interface dead. No assist. No overlays. Street signage was dumb without layers, just arrows and static maps and rain collecting in cracked lightboxes. It should have felt clean. Instead it felt like she was moving through a city designed for everyone except the version of herself she kept defending.

Ava’s text came twenty-three minutes after she got inside.

You seemed tired tonight.
Maybe you hold too tight all the time.

Then, a moment later:

I’m not pushing. I mean it kindly.

The message sat on the screen in that small patient font designed to make everything feel manageable. No accusation. No trap. That was almost the worst part. The kindness.

Lia set the phone face down on the counter and stood in the middle of her apartment listening to the silence. Usually she loved it here. No ambient feed in the walls, no adaptive scent packets, no domestic assistant chirping reminders into the corners. Just the old refrigerator hum, the radiator ticking, the occasional tires whispering through wet streets below. Chosen calm. Her kind of luxury.

Tonight the quiet had weight.

It pressed from the ceiling. It collected in the angles of the room. The apartment stopped feeling like shelter and started feeling like a demonstration model of restraint, a clean little box where a woman could live for years without ever disturbing anyone else’s picture of her. She looked at the books squared on the shelf, the keys in the ceramic dish, the coat on its hook. Everything exactly where she had put it. Everything loyal. Everything waiting for her to keep being the same person forever.

Maybe Ava was wrong. Maybe Ava was exactly right. Either way, Lia could not breathe in that room without feeling measured.

She left with no plan.

No overlay halo, no route optimization, no emergency tether quietly plotting the safest walk home before she had even gone anywhere. She put on her coat, took the stairs instead of the lift, and stepped into the night with the stubborn pulse of someone who needed to do one thing unscripted before dawn or lose her nerve.

Rain had thinned to a mist that hovered under the streetlights like static. The city at that hour was all thresholds: laundries still open, delivery bays breathing diesel, noodle counters steaming behind fogged glass, men in workwear smoking under phosphor awnings while their rented scooters blinked in patient rows. Her shoes darkened at the toes. A tram slid past full of faces lit from below by private weather.

Lia walked until choice itself started to feel less theoretical. Left here. Right there. Stop at the crossing, then don’t. No system smoothing the path, no green band rising in her vision to tell her which turn would save two minutes and lower cortisol by three points. It was messy. Good. For the first time all day she felt a live wire under her ribs. Not peace. Proof.

She found the bar by accident or by appetite. Hard to say.

It was half underground, marked only by a flickering blue rectangle over a stairwell and the bass-thud leaking through concrete. No reputation feed hovered over the entrance. No customer score. No curated queue. Just wet steps, sticker scars on the railing, a smell from below that promised old beer and electrical fatigue.

Perfect, she thought, which should have warned her.

Inside, the room was low-ceilinged and badly balanced. One speaker was blown, so the left side of every song rasped like a damaged throat. A strip light above the back shelves blinked out every fourteen seconds, then returned with a migraine-green pulse. People were packed close in mismatched coats, talking too loud, moving without the glossy synchronization she hated in cleaner places. A woman laughed with her whole spine. Someone dropped a glass and nobody paused long enough to make etiquette out of it.

For a few minutes, it worked on her.

Lia stood at the bar with a drink she barely tasted and watched the room refuse coherence. No optimization. No soft choreography. No invisible hand feathering everyone’s edges into something marketable. Disorder had a smell, sour and human and immediate. She felt herself loosen by millimeters. Maybe this was all she had needed. A place ugly enough not to ask anything of her.

Then she noticed the man near the pillar.

Not because he was remarkable. He wasn’t. Mid-thirties maybe, dark jacket, tired face, the kind of body built by carrying things for money. But his attention had a fixedness to it. He wasn’t looking around the room and landing on her sometimes. He was looking at her and letting the room happen around that.

Lia turned away. Counted to ten. Looked back. Still there.

Attention has temperature. She felt the room change before he moved.

She put the glass down and headed for the stairwell, meaning to leave before the night could decide to become something else. She had gotten what she came for. A break in pattern. Enough.

He stepped in close near the bottom of the stairs.

“Hey,” he said, like they were already in the middle of a conversation.

Lia angled past him. “No.”

She got one step. Then his hand closed around her elbow.

The grip was practiced in the ugliest way: casual on the surface, corrective underneath, like he was not grabbing her so much as adjusting her direction. Lia jerked free on instinct.

“Don’t touch me.”

He smiled without warmth. “Relax.”

His hand caught her wrist this time.

Everything in her went cold and bright. She pulled back hard. He tightened. Not playful. Not mistaken. She said no again, louder, and his body moved closer as if the word were just weather he had decided to walk through.

The stairwell narrowed behind them. Concrete wall to one side, metal rail to the other, bodies passing close at the bar entrance but not close enough, not attentive enough. Music beat through the floor. Somebody shouted over a joke upstairs. The world kept its pace.

Lia tried to twist her wrist free. He switched angles, using her resistance to reel her in. His shoulder cut off the steps. His breath hit her cheek, stale and hot.

“Stop,” she said.

He tugged at her collar with the hand that wasn’t on her wrist, dragging the fabric sideways hard enough to bite her throat. The move was so sudden it stunned her more than the force itself. Not seduction. Control. The difference was total.

She shoved at his chest. He didn’t move enough.

He crowded her back into the rail.

Metal struck her spine. The air went wrong. For a second the room folded down to contact points: his fingers grinding her wrist, fabric twisted at her neck, the cold bar of the railing digging between her shoulders, the awful administrative calm in his face. This was happening. No ambiguity left in it. No accidental brush to misread, no social static to hide inside. He was doing this. He knew he was doing this. He expected the world to make room.

Lia turned her head and said no again, harsher now, because language was the only thing she still had fully under her command. He leaned in closer, crowding even that.

Then something broke somewhere behind him.

A tray hit the floor. A man swore. Glass shattered. At almost the same instant the strip lights over the bar cut out, plunging half the room into a dirty pulse of darkness. Bodies surged sideways on reflex, not panic exactly, just that fast animal adjustment crowds make when the pattern tears. Someone slammed into the man’s shoulder.

His grip slipped.

Lia ripped her wrist free, ducked under his arm, and ran.

Up the stairs, out through the wet mouth of the alley, into cold air sharp enough to hurt. She did not look back. She did not stop to orient. Her body had already chosen the only viable algorithm: distance, distance, distance.

She walked three blocks before realizing she was shaking hard enough to miss steps.

Her wrist throbbed where he’d held it. Her elbow felt branded. The collar of her coat scraped her neck and she almost tore the thing off right there under a transit camera. Every time someone passed too near behind her, her shoulders locked. Every reflection in every dark shop window turned into a check. Is he there. Is anyone. What is that shape. Go faster.

By the time she reached her building, the city had become pure threat geometry: doorways, blind corners, elevator mirrors, the lag between hearing footsteps and locating them. The same streets that had felt gloriously unmanaged an hour earlier now looked like a circuit board designed by someone who hated women and loved plausible deniability.

Inside her apartment, she locked the door twice and still stood with her hand on the latch for a full minute, listening.

Then she went straight to the shower and turned the water too hot.

Steam filled the room fast, blurring the mirror until her face disappeared. Lia stripped and stepped under the stream before it settled, heat hitting her shoulders like punishment she had assigned herself. She scrubbed at her wrist first, then her neck where the collar had bitten, then both arms as if memory could be removed by abrasion. Soap. Water. Again. Harder.

It did nothing.

His grip stayed. The tug at her collar stayed. Worst was the moment at the rail, the short ugly instant when breathing had turned contractual and she had not been the one writing the terms. She kept replaying it in broken loops, as if the mind believed repetition might uncover an exit it had missed the first time.

When the water cooled, she made it hot again.

Afterward she sat on the bathroom floor in a towel, skin burning, hair dripping down her back, and watched the fog thin from the mirror in slow vertical tears. The apartment was silent except for the pipes. Chosen calm, she thought, and almost laughed. The room had not protected her. The self she had built inside it had not protected her. All that control, all that careful abstention, and one night off-script had been enough to show her what the city’s rougher edges thought a woman alone was for.

She did not sleep.

At 05:12 she lay in bed staring at the ceiling while a sanitation drone whined somewhere below. At 06:03 she got up because remaining horizontal started to feel like surrender. At 07:10 muscle memory took her to the café anyway. Not because she wanted company exactly. More because the room had held her shape for years: the same window table, the same sightline to the street, Marco’s familiar nod, the little civic fiction that a life can stay assembled if it repeats correctly enough.

Morning light made the place look gentler than it was. Chrome. Pastry gloss. A queue of commuters carrying their private feeds around their heads like invisible halos. Marco glanced up when she came in, gave her the small two-finger salute that usually steadied her, and set her usual cup on the table before she reached it.

This morning even that ritual failed to land clean.

The grinder kicked on and she flinched so hard she nearly sloshed coffee over her hand. Steam burst from the wand and her chest locked. Every reflective surface in the room kept catching her eye: the pastry case, the black register screen, the window at street level. She scanned them without meaning to, searching for shapes behind her. A customer laughed too sharply and she jolted hard enough to turn heads.

The golden-girl performance had always relied on tempo. Smile here. Small joke. Steady hands around the cup. Make calm look natural. This morning her body refused the script with blunt mechanical honesty. Her nerves were all exposed wire.

Marco noticed by the second time she startled.

He didn’t call attention to it. He just waited until the line thinned, came out from behind the counter with a glass of water she hadn’t asked for, and set it beside her cup.

“Hey,” he said, quiet. “You want me to tell people this table’s taken for a while?”

Lia opened her mouth to tell him she was fine and felt the lie catch like glass halfway up. Marco watched her without crowding, the way you might watch an animal that has come out from under debris and doesn’t know yet whether help is another kind of trap.

“Bathroom,” she said.

Marco nodded once. “Take your time.”

No questions. No theory. Just space. She could have thanked him in a voice that would have sounded like breaking, and hated the thought as soon as it arrived.

In the café bathroom, under a fluorescent strip that buzzed with old-ballast fatigue, Lia stood with both palms on the sink and waited for the room to stop tilting. Her phone felt too light in her hand.

Ava had texted again an hour earlier.

You okay?
You seemed off when I left you last night.

Lia stared at the message until the words blurred. Concern, still. Real concern. Not a pitch this time. Not exactly. But concern has its own intimacy, and right now intimacy felt like a hand reaching toward a bruise.

Her thumb moved anyway.

She typed: Just underslept.
Deleted it.

She typed: Long night. I’m good.
Deleted that too.

For a second she considered a joke, something with enough dry static in it to make the whole thing slide back into her usual register. She could hear herself doing it. Could practically watch the mechanism engage. Minimize. Deflect. Keep the line clean. Keep everybody else from having to revise the version of Lia that made them feel stable.

Outside the door, cups knocked together. The milk fridge compressor kicked on. Somewhere out in the room Marco laughed at something a customer said, but even that sound came to her as if through water.

Lia looked down at her wrist. There was no dramatic bruise yet, just a faint reddening where fingers had been. The kind of mark the world could easily ask her to ignore. The kind of mark that invited self-gaslighting if you were practiced enough. She knew that trick. She had lived inside tidier versions of it for years.

Not this time.

The words arrived plain. No scaffolding. No elegance. Just a breach in the wall.

I’m not okay.
