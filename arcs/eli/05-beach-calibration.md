Beach Calibration

The water helped.

Not because it was real. Because it was moving.

Elia walked down to where the tide kept thinning itself across the sand and let it wash over her feet once, then again. The first shock of coolness climbed up through her legs and settled something in her chest for half a second. She stood there longer than she meant to, looking out at the line where the light broke apart on the surface.

Behind her, the beach kept doing what it had clearly been built to do. Drinks arriving before hands lifted too far from the armrest. Towels folded exactly where someone would reach for them. People moving around one another without ever quite colliding. A place that wanted to feel casual badly enough to rehearse it.

She walked back up the sand and picked one of the empty chairs near the waterline.

The cover-up clung slightly at the backs of her thighs when she sat. Her hair had started drying in loose waves across one shoulder. Somewhere farther inland, music moved under the sound of the surf, too low to count as a song, too persistent to be an accident.

She closed her eyes for a minute.

When she opened them again, a shadow had stopped beside the empty chair to her right.

"Am I interrupting something important," a man asked, "or just high-end avoidance."

She looked up.

He had sunglasses in one hand and a drink in the other. Mid-thirties maybe. Easy face. Tan that looked earned instead of purchased. He was dressed just this side of careless, which she suspected had taken work.

"That depends," she said. "Do you make a habit of sitting down before you get an answer."

He smiled. "Only when I think the answer's probably yes."

"That's a terrible habit."

"It gets mixed reviews."

He stayed standing long enough that refusing still felt available.

Elia looked at the chair, then back at him. "Go ahead."

He sat with the sort of confidence that only worked because he wasn't pushing it.

"Marcus," he said.

"Elia."

He said her name back without the tiny stutter she had started bracing for from strangers. "Good to meet you."

That caught her.

"You didn't do the thing," she said.

"What thing."

"The extra second." She shrugged one shoulder. "The little internal paperwork."

Marcus looked at her over the rim of his glass. "You seem disappointed."

"I'm suspicious."

"That's fair."

He didn't ask what she meant. That helped.

A server passed behind them with a tray balanced one-handed. Marcus lifted two fingers without looking up. The server nodded once and kept walking.

Elia noticed.

"So you know people here."

"Enough to avoid the bad drinks."

"That sounds practiced."

"It is."

A minute later a second glass appeared on the small table between them.

Elia looked up at the server. "I didn't order anything."

The server smiled politely. "You don't have to."

Then they were gone.

She looked down at the drink and laughed under her breath. "That's normal."

"For here?" Marcus took a sip of his own. "Pretty much."

The drink was biting cold. Something citrus, something sharp, something bitter at the back that kept it from going soft. She hated that it was exactly what she wanted.

Marcus noticed and was decent enough not to say so.

They sat for a while without talking.

Not awkward. Not easy either. Just two people looking at the water while a whole machine arranged itself around their quiet.

Finally he said, "First week?"

That was almost gentle enough to be annoying.

"Is it that obvious."

"Only if you've seen it before."

She turned toward him. "Seen what before."

He shrugged. "The look."

"What look is that."

"You keep relaxing and then remembering where you are."

Elia stared at him.

He lifted one shoulder. "Was I wrong."

No. He wasn't.

That irritated her on principle.

"What about you," she asked. "You've been here long enough to diagnose strangers from twenty feet away."

Marcus smiled a little into his glass. "Long enough."

"That's not an answer."

"It's the one I'm using."

She should have left it there. Instead she heard herself ask, "Do you live here."

He tipped his head, considering the word. "Most of the time."

Again: technically an answer. Also not one.

The breeze shifted. The music from inland rose for a second, then dropped again. A couple farther down the row stood to leave, and two attendants appeared from nowhere to fold their towels before the chairs had fully cooled.

Elia watched that happen.

"You ever get tired of how much this place wants to help," she asked.

"Yes."

The answer came too fast to be decorative.

That surprised her enough to make her look back at him.

He was already watching the water again.

"You're not going to give me the brochure version," she said.

"No."

"Why."

He took another sip. "You look like you'd throw it back at me."

That got a laugh out of her before she could stop it.

Marcus looked pleased with himself for maybe half a second. Not more.

She asked, "How's that line usually work."

"It doesn't." He stretched one leg out in the sand. "But it bought me another minute."

"Bold of you to assume you had one."

"I'm still here."

That was true. Also annoying.

The server came back and replaced the melting ice in their glasses without asking.

Elia watched them go. "I'm trying to decide whether everybody here is terrifying or just overtrained."

"Those aren't mutually exclusive."

"Good. Glad somebody said it."

He looked at her then, properly. Not a long stare. Just long enough to make the next question feel deliberate.

"How's the body."

She answered too quickly. "Fine."

Marcus waited.

Elia looked down at her own hand where it rested against the side of the glass. Different wrist. Different knuckles. Nails that still startled her a little when the light caught them.

"Better than fine," she said.

He nodded once. "Yeah."

That was all.

No soft congratulations. No little speech about authenticity or becoming or any of the words everybody else seemed to think would land well on a person three days into permanent residency. Just that one quiet acknowledgment.

It got under her skin.

She said, "You ask that like you know what the answer costs."

Marcus looked down at his drink. "I ask it like I know people lie about it when they don't."

A woman passed with a towel over one shoulder and looked once at Marcus, then again at Elia. Not rude. Just too quick to be nothing.

Elia noticed that too.

"People here seem to know what to do with you," she said.

Marcus laughed softly. "That happens."

"Doesn't bother you?"

"Sometimes."

The sun had dropped a little lower. The light on the water was warmer now, flatter. Behind them the beach got busier without feeling any louder. Someone's laughter carried cleanly over the surf. Somewhere near the umbrellas, glass clicked against glass.

Marcus set his sunglasses on the table and leaned back in the chair. "You always this suspicious."

"Only when things are too easy."

"That must make life here unbearable."

She looked out at the water again. "You'd think."

He let that sit.

For the first time since she'd arrived, Elia became aware of how her body was arranged in the chair: shoulder tipped back, knees angled toward the water, one foot half-buried in sand. Nothing dramatic about it. It just wasn't defensive. She had been that way for almost twenty minutes without noticing.

That realization bothered her more than Marcus did.

He said, "You can leave, you know."

She gave him a look. "That's not how this place feels."

"No," he said. "Probably not."

That answer did more for him than anything else had. Not because it solved anything. Because he hadn't rushed to fix it.

The beach kept arranging itself around them. Towels folding. Drinks refreshing. Shade holding its exact line.

Elia sat with the surf in front of her and Marcus beside her and felt, with fresh irritation, how long she had already stayed.

For now, that was answer enough.
