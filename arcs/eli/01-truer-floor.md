Truer Floor

The elevator opened on time.

Damien stepped into the corridor and let the doors close behind him. His assistant was still trying to keep pace, tablet in both hands, voice controlled enough to pass for calm.

"Three investor calls moved up. Pacific habitats wants legal review on the licensing discrepancy. Sato is asking for revised language before the board packet goes out. And Mercer's memo is on top."

"Of course it is."

She glanced at the tablet. "He marked it urgent."

Damien kept walking. The corridor was glass on one side, polished concrete on the other, the whole floor built to make hesitation look out of place. "Jonathan Mercer marks his conscience urgent every time it inconveniences him."

"He's asking for expanded oversight on adaptive-identity environments. Independent review, exit audits, mandatory-"

Damien stopped just long enough to turn his head.

She fell silent.

He never needed volume. "Leave the memo. Delay the rest."

By the time he reached his office, the city beyond the glass had reduced itself to towers, traffic, and river-light. From this height, even disorder found a pattern.

He set the tablet on the desk and opened Mercer's memo.

The language was exactly what he expected. Precise. Conscientious. Annoyingly clean. Jonathan had not accused anyone of wrongdoing. He was too disciplined for that. Instead he had assembled a case for restraint. More scrutiny on adaptive immersion users. Stronger review before permanent residency conversions. Separation of therapeutic environments from revenue incentives. Human oversight wherever prolonged immersion and identity drift began to overlap.

Jonathan Mercer wrote like a man trying to brace a door before the first real impact hit it.

Damien read once, then cast the memo aside. A personnel map opened above the desk. Mercer's file sat at center. Beneath it, a smaller node brightened.

Eli Mercer.

The profile expanded.

Student. Campus housing. Bookstore shifts. Grades uneven but recoverable. Immersion hours high enough to worry a father and low enough to avoid formal intervention. Sleep erosion. Disengagement volatility. Adaptive-body experimentation kept private because Eli still imagined privacy had sharp enough walls to hold.

Damien leaned back.

Not a weakness, exactly. Jonathan was too controlled to be compromised by ordinary family tension. But pressure did not need to break a man to be useful. Sometimes it only needed to divide his attention, slow his judgment, make the wrong question personal at the wrong moment.

He rotated Eli's profile. Session timings. Campus log-ins. Residency pages opened and closed before completion. Identity queries with the edges clipped by privacy layers. The pattern was obvious. Eli was already leaning toward something he had not yet allowed himself to name without qualification.

Damien opened the hidden partition beside the shelving with a key no biometric system had ever touched. Dark channels lifted across the wall. A secure line opened. No ringtone. No greeting.

Shinobi answered in silence.

Damien pushed Eli's profile into the channel. "I have a pressure point."

The silence on the other end changed. Not much. Enough.

"Jonathan Mercer's son," Damien said. "Already deep in the adaptive layers. Already unstable in the useful way."

"You want a seduction operation."

"No. Seduction implies invention." Damien looked back at the profile. "This one has already found the door. I want life outside it to stop feeling dependable."

A short pause.

"Parameters?"

"Nothing loud. No obvious obstruction. Administrative friction. Timing drift. Small embarrassments. Make ordinary life less forgiving and let the contrast do the rest."

"You're buying preference."

"I'm stripping away the lie that preference forms in clean air."

Below the glass, traffic moved in measured streams. Every car looked self-directed from a distance.

Shinobi said, "Why him?"

"Because Jonathan is pushing oversight, and oversight gets moral confidence from proximity. Strip that confidence out and he spends the next quarter trying to decide whether he's drafting policy or begging for his child in professional language." Damien let the file turn once more. "And because the son is already asking the right dangerous questions."

"Dangerous for whom?"

"For anyone still pretending freedom exists outside architecture."

The line held for another second.

Then Shinobi said, "You want it to feel self-chosen."

"Yes."

The channel closed.

Damien stood alone with the city, Jonathan's memo, and the smaller life pulsing beneath it. Eli was not important yet. That would have to be earned. For now he was leverage, and a way to see how much institutional nerve Jonathan Mercer actually had once theory acquired a face.

On the desk, the memo remained open to a paragraph about consent.

Damien shut it with one finger.

Cooling fans kept a low hum under the violet screens in Shinobi's room. Cables ran in exact lines. The Neurodex case sat closed beside the console, matte black and untouched.

He brought Eli Mercer up on the center display and let the details populate.

Campus dining history. Housing access. Bookstore shift rosters. Advising notes. Small outstanding charges. A half-finished application packet Eli had opened six times in eleven days and never submitted. Friends who tagged him in late-night photos, then less and less often. The outline of a life that still looked ordinary if you read quickly.

He opened three administrative channels and worked without hurry.

A bursar notice that should have cleared weeks ago resurfaced as unresolved. A professor's acknowledgment email slipped into a queue where neglect and interference looked identical. Two bookstore shifts moved just enough to scrape against office hours and an advising appointment Eli had been trying to keep. A housing form stalled in a review bucket reserved for entries missing one invisible field.

Nothing theatrical. That was amateur thinking. The goal was not to make him feel hunted. The goal was to make him feel unlucky, then tired, then faintly ashamed by how much a few small disruptions could throw him.

Shinobi watched the changes settle.

He could have done more. Broken locks instead of nudging hinges. There were faster ways to push a life off its axis. Damien had asked for pressure, not spectacle. That part Shinobi respected. Force announced itself. A field of inconveniences did not. It simply trained the body to notice where relief lived.

A small alert opened in the corner of the screen. Eli's latest adaptive-session log. Shorter than the previous one. Terminated manually. Reopened forty-two minutes later.

Shinobi expanded the metadata just enough to see the pattern and not the content. The privacy wall shimmered, then held. Fine. He did not need the body map yet. The timing told him enough. Eli was not being dragged toward something alien. He was circling something he wanted and feared in the same motion.

That made the work easier and meaner.

He closed the log and left the Neurodex case where it was.

Across the city, Eli Mercer woke to the tone of a tablet alert and knew before opening his eyes that something else had gone slightly wrong.

The room had the same tired shape it always did. Narrow bed. Desk under the window. Two mugs, one rinsed and one not. Sweatshirt hanging off the chair like it had given up trying to be folded. Colorless morning light through the blinds.

The tone sounded again.

He rolled over and woke the screen.

BALANCE DUE: $84.12
Failure to resolve may result in holds on registration and campus services.

He stared at the number until irritation turned to doubt.

He had paid that. Hadn't he?

He sat up, thumbed through old receipts, found the confirmation screen from three weeks ago, and felt his shoulders tighten anyway. The payment code was there. The green checkmark was there. The account still said overdue.

"Great," he said to the empty room.

By the time he got dressed and crossed campus to the bursar office, the line was already long enough to feel punitive. He checked the time twice. If he stayed, he would miss the first part of class. If he left, the hold might hit the bookstore direct deposit, and he did not have enough faith in the university to test how far its incompetence extended.

When he finally reached the desk, the woman there looked at his screen, then at hers, then back at his.

"It's probably a sync issue," she said.

"It says there's a hold."

"Right." She gave him the practiced smile of someone paid to stay calm while being useless. "It should clear once the system refreshes."

"When?"

Another smile. Smaller. "Soon."

By the time he made it to class, the lecture had already started. He slipped into the back row and opened his notes to find a message from the bookstore manager pinned at the top of the page.

Schedule update. Need you in at two instead of six. Also added Saturday morning. Check portal.

He closed his eyes for a second.

At two, he had his advising appointment.

At six, he was supposed to meet his father for dinner.

He opened the portal. The shift change had been made eleven minutes earlier. No contest option without manager approval. He almost laughed. It would have felt dramatic if it had not been so small.

At the front of the room, the professor was saying something about continuity in narrative systems. Everyone else seemed to be following it without effort. Eli wrote three lines, looked down a minute later, and could not remember writing any of them.

The day never quite broke. It just refused to settle.

The bookstore's back scanner rejected his access card on the first try and accepted it on the second. A customer insisted on using an expired student discount code and then looked at him as though Eli had written the policy himself. During break, his manager stared at the roster and said, "Huh," in the flat voice of someone mildly inconvenienced by her own system.

"I had you on nights this week."

"You had me on two nights," Eli said.

She kept looking at the screen. "Now I don't."

He wanted to ask whether that was supposed to be funny. Instead he took the stack of returned texts from her and spent twenty minutes shelving neuroscience primers and intro-to-law casebooks while the same thought kept catching in his head: none of this was big enough to complain about.

That was the part that got under his skin.

By the time he got off shift, he had missed advising entirely. The reschedule link spun for twelve seconds, then returned an error. He tried again. Same result.

Outside, the pavement still held the rain in thin reflective sheets. Campus lights burned in cold rows. Students moved past in small groups with the easy composure of people whose lives were still holding together in public.

His phone buzzed.

Dad: Still on for 7?

Eli looked at the message, looked away, typed yes, deleted it, then sent On my way.

The restaurant light made everyone look audited.

Jonathan Mercer was already seated when Eli arrived, sleeves rolled, water untouched, expression arranged into patience. The place was one of Jonathan's compromises: not formal enough to make dinner feel strategic, not casual enough to let either of them forget who was trying to set the terms.

Eli came in three minutes late on purpose. Petty, but it let him alter one thing in a day that kept moving without asking him.

Jonathan stood when he reached the table.

"You look tired," he said.

"So do you."

A brief almost-smile, then they sat.

For a minute they talked about work, classes, a faculty strike rumor Jonathan had seen on the feed. Eli let the conversation drift because he knew where it was headed and because postponement still counted as a form of control in his head.

Jonathan waited until the server left with their order.

"The compliance internship is still open," he said.

Eli leaned back and looked past him at the mirror behind the bar, where the whole room seemed flatter and farther away. "Not my thing."

"It's paid."

"That isn't the same thing."

"It's stable."

Eli gave a short laugh. "That's closer."

Jonathan folded his hands once on the table. "You need something with structure, Eli."

The word need hit him harder than it should have. The whole day had been speaking that language. Need to resolve. Need to confirm. Need to update.

"I have a job."

"At a bookstore."

"Which still counts as a job."

Jonathan's face tightened, then eased. He was always editing himself in real time. "I'm not belittling it."

"You kind of are."

"I'm trying to point you toward something that leads somewhere."

Eli looked down at his own hands. Chipped dark polish on two nails. Ink near the thumb from the register. Hands that always seemed to belong to someone caught between explanations.

"Maybe I'm already going somewhere."

Jonathan was quiet for a beat. "Into Virtualia?"

The server set down their plates and disappeared before either of them had to answer. Jonathan waited until she was gone.

"I checked the family dashboard," he said.

Eli stared at him. "Why?"

"Because I'm your father."

"That's not actually an answer."

Jonathan's jaw shifted. "Your immersion hours are up. Your disengagement patterns are-"

"My what?"

"Eli."

"No, say it. My disengagement patterns are what?"

Jonathan lowered his voice instead of raising it. That made the whole exchange feel more controlled and somehow more humiliating. "Concerning."

Eli pushed his fork through food he had already lost interest in. "You monitored my sessions."

"I checked a dashboard tied to family safety permissions."

"That you left active after I turned nineteen."

"To make sure you were all right."

Jonathan leaned forward slightly. "I'm not doing this to police you."

"Then why does it keep feeling like paperwork with your face on it?"

The line landed. Jonathan sat back.

For a moment he looked older than Eli was used to, not because of gray or fatigue but because the concern had stopped wearing its professional mask. "Because I know what these systems do when they stop being toys."

"It's not a toy."

"That is exactly what worries me."

Eli could have lied then. Could have shrugged and called it stress relief, distraction, a game. Something clean enough for Jonathan to oppose without having to understand it. Instead he said nothing, because silence still felt less exposing than the truth.

The truth was that Virtualia did not feel fake. It did not even feel like escape, not exactly. It felt like some knot in him loosened there without asking permission. Like a room inside his own body stopped clenching. Like the part of life spent translating himself into acceptable terms briefly went quiet.

He could not say any of that across a table set with polished silver and water glasses and his father's careful attention.

Jonathan took his silence for resistance, maybe for shame. "I'm trying to help you keep a foothold in the real world."

"There it is," Eli said.

"What?"

"The real world."

Jonathan frowned. "You say that like it's an insult."

"I say it like you only bring it up when you want me to choose whatever version of myself makes you less nervous."

Jonathan held still. "That isn't fair."

"Maybe not."

"It isn't."

Eli looked at him. Jonathan's tie was slightly off-center. He had probably come straight from the office. He looked tired in the honest way, not the performed one. Under all the control there was something rawer. Fear, maybe. Or the long habit of turning fear into a form other people would respect.

That should have made Eli soften. Instead it made the whole thing sadder.

Jonathan said, more quietly now, "I know you think I want you to be simple."

Eli laughed once without humor. "Do you?"

"No."

"Recognizable, then."

Jonathan's expression changed by a degree. "Recognizable is not the insult you think it is."

"It is when it's the only shape that gets called safe."

Neither of them moved.

Then Jonathan said, "I'm trying to protect your future."

No anger. No accusation. Just love translated into structure.

Eli looked down at the plate between them. "From what?"

Jonathan answered too quickly. "From making permanent decisions inside systems built to erase consequence."

Eli could not tell him that the erasure sometimes seemed to happen outside first. That the approved version of his life often felt less like freedom than a supervised audition for manhood, career, legibility, all the things Jonathan kept calling stability. He could not tell him that in Virtualia the most unsettling thing was not fantasy, but recognition.

So he reached for the safer cruelty.

"Maybe you just don't like where the future points."

Jonathan absorbed that without flinching, which made it worse. "Maybe I don't like any institution that profits when confused people mistake relief for truth."

Confused.

The word landed so neatly Eli almost admired it.

He set his fork down. "I'm not confused."

Jonathan's answer came softer than before. "Then don't act like someone waiting for a machine to decide who he is."

Eli was on his feet before he had fully decided to stand.

Jonathan looked up at him, startled now. "Eli-"

"I said I'd think about the internship," Eli said. "That's apparently not enough."

He grabbed his jacket.

Jonathan didn't reach for him. He only said, "Sit down."

"Why?"

"Because leaving in the middle of the conversation doesn't make you more certain."

"No," Eli said. "It just gets me out of your version of me for five minutes."

That sat between them.

Jonathan's expression did not harden. That would have been easier. Instead something in him folded inward. "I came here to help."

Eli stood there with his jacket half on and hated how true that sounded.

"I know," he said.

Then he left anyway.

Outside, the night was wet and bright with traffic. He walked two blocks before checking his phone.

No new messages. No dramatic follow-up. Just the city and the ache he always carried after arguments with his father, because Jonathan never made it clean enough to hate him without residue.

Back in his room, he kicked off his shoes and sat at the desk without turning on the overhead light. The campus outside had gone dim and blue. Somewhere down the hall, somebody laughed too loudly at something he would never hear the setup for.

His tablet buzzed once.

Dad: I came on too hard. I know that.
Dad: I'm not trying to make you smaller.
Dad: I'm trying to make sure you still have choices when this passes.

Eli stared at the last line.

When this passes.

As if whatever was happening in him had the neat temporary body of a fever. As if time alone could return him to the shape his father kept trying to protect.

Another buzz.

Dad: The internship isn't punishment. It's structure. Structure keeps doors open.

Eli set the tablet facedown.

On the desk, beside a half-finished class reading and two receipts he had meant to throw away, the Virtualia icon glowed in the corner of his screen. He had not opened it. The machine had simply remembered him more faithfully than the rest of the day had.

He looked at it for a long moment.

Then he touched the icon.

The interface unfolded in soft phosphor layers. No alarms. No metrics. No one asking him to verify, confirm, resolve, or reschedule. Just the patient architecture of return.

Welcome back.

He almost closed it again on reflex. The argument with Jonathan was still under his skin, and part of him wanted to spite every system in sight by refusing all of them at once. But refusal took more shape than he had left tonight. He was tired in the deep way, the kind that made thought feel expensive.

He entered.

The load sequence dissolved into a twilight street he had used before, one of the quieter districts with rain-slick stone and lit windows and a sky held between evening and neon. He stood there for a moment and felt the difference arrive not as ecstasy but as release. His shoulders lowered. His breathing changed. Some constant tension in his body, one he had stopped noticing until it disappeared, let go.

That frightened him more than the glamour ever could.

Far ahead, a reflective panel caught his silhouette and returned it altered by the profile he had been adjusting in private for weeks now. Softer lines. A different center of gravity. Hair that fell the way he kept telling himself was only aesthetic curiosity. The figure in the glass was not finished. Not final. But she was there enough to make his chest tighten.

Not invention.

Recognition.

He walked closer.

The surface brightened around him and the avatar sharpened by degrees. This version of him did not feel like costume. Costume was what happened all day outside, the constant labor of being interpreted before he spoke. Here the labor thinned. Here the body stopped arguing with the room.

He lifted one hand and watched her do the same.

For a second the whole day-the hold notice, the shift change, the missed advising appointment, the dinner with Jonathan and its careful injuries-fell away so completely it was almost frightening. The world outside had spent twelve hours making small corrections to his life. In here, nothing corrected him. Nothing needed him translated first.

A prompt pulsed at the edge of the glass.

Resume adaptive profile?

Eli stood there with his hand half-raised, looking at the version of himself waiting on the other side of the prompt.

He clicked yes.

The body in the reflection adjusted. Not much. Enough.

Something inside him, some line braced for years, went quiet with a force that felt dangerously close to grief.

Outside, his phone buzzed once more against the desk in the dark room he had just left behind.

He did not go back for it.
