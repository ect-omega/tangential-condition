The shoes fit as neatly as the dress had.

Elia sat on the edge of the bed and fastened the straps without hurrying, then stood and waited a second longer than she needed to. The access panel beside the door was still lit. Same floor. Same small green arrow.

No new instruction. No note. Just the route holding itself open.

She picked up the black card, slipped it into her hand, and left the suite.

The corridor outside was empty and softly lit, carpet dark enough to swallow footfall. Somewhere farther down, an elevator arrived with a low mechanical sigh and stood there waiting before she had fully reached it.

Of course.

Inside, the mirrored wall gave her back the dress in fragments. Red at the waist. Red at the shoulder. A mouth set flatter than she meant it to be. She looked away before she could start adjusting things that did not need adjusting.

The private floor opened onto a quieter antechamber than she expected. Dark stone. Low light. A console desk with nobody behind it until a woman in black appeared from a side door and moved toward her with the smoothness of someone trained not to startle.

“Good evening,” the woman said. “This way.”

No name. No question about whether Elia intended to attend. Just the next door held open.

She followed.

The sound reached her before the room did. Glass against glass. Music low enough to imply money. The soft rise and fall of voices performing ease at a high level.

Then the doors opened and the gala took her in.

It was warmer than the corridor and colder than a normal room should have been, the air held in some expensive balance between perfume, ice, and polished wood. Chandelier light fractured across glassware and the black windows beyond, where the city looked flattened into red lines and white towers.

Nobody turned all at once.

That would have been kinder.

Instead attention found her in increments. One face, then another. A pause in a conversation near the bar. A woman by the windows taking in the dress first, then the face, then recalculating. Somewhere farther in, someone smiled too quickly and then looked over Elia’s shoulder for the person who must have brought her.

He was waiting near the glass.

Damien in this room wore himself differently than Marcus had. Same body. Same face. No beach looseness left in it. The easier register had not disappeared exactly; it had just been put away with the daylight.

“You came,” he said.

“You sound surprised.”

“I sound updated.”

That was probably the closest thing to warmth she was getting.

His gaze moved over the dress once, not lingering. Not complimenting. Just registering what the room would already have seen by now.

“The color works,” he said.

Elia looked down at herself, then back at him. “That’s one way to put it.”

“It’s an accurate one.”

He held out a glass taken from a passing tray. She took it because refusing would have been its own speech.

The first sip tasted dry and bitter and expensive.

Damien turned slightly, just enough to bring her into the angle of his body without touching her. “Stay close until you know where the exits aren’t.”

She looked at him. “That’s a horrible sentence.”

“It’s also useful.”

The room seemed to understand that she had arrived before she did. Space shifted. Not enough to call it choreography. Enough that no one blocked their line through the floor. As Damien started walking, she found herself moving with him because standing still would have been louder.

He introduced her to no one.

That was its own strategy.

People approached anyway. A man with a shaved head and a smile too sharp to trust told Damien he’d been meaning to call and let his eyes linger on Elia half a second too long. A woman in silver said, “You look extraordinary,” and somehow made it sound like an assessment of market conditions. Another woman asked Elia whether she was “finding continuity comfortable,” as if they were discussing a resort stay and not a permanent change in state.

Elia answered in short, flat lines and watched the room keep trying to sort her.

It was not curiosity, not really. Curiosity had edges of uncertainty to it. This was closer to placement. She could feel people deciding what category would let them relax around her fastest. Success story. Risk contained. Mercer complication resolved into something photogenic.

Every glance seemed to ask the same ugly question in a different register: if she could stand here composed, dressed, visible, then how bad could permanence really be.

Then she heard it, not from Damien, but from two men half-turned away near the center of the floor.

“…Mercer’s—”
“I know who she is.”
“Well, she looks intact to me.”

The second man said it with the confidence of somebody announcing the result of a test he had not been forced to take.

There it was.

Elia kept walking and felt the cost of the dress land properly for the first time. Not just elegance. Not just pressure. Presentation. She had been placed in the room to say something before she opened her mouth. The room was already reading her beside Damien and building the sentence it wanted.

Jonathan’s child.
Chosen permanence.
No visible damage.
Case closed.

Her grip tightened once around the stem of the glass.

Damien noticed, but only by a fraction.

He brought her to the quieter edge of the room near the windows, where the city light cut up their reflections and laid them over the skyline.

“Better,” he said.

“Than what.”

“The center.”

She looked back over her shoulder. “They’re still looking.”

“Yes.”

“That doesn’t seem to bother you.”

“Looking is cheap.”

She turned toward him. “And what’s expensive.”

“Conclusions.”

That one sat between them.

Across the room, a woman lowered her phone after what was almost certainly a picture. A man near the bar shifted his whole posture when he realized Damien had seen him do it. People were not being subtle now. They thought they were. That was different.

Elia said, “You could have warned me.”

“I did.”

“In the corridor.”

“Yes.”

“That barely counts.”

“It counted enough for you to come anyway.”

She hated how little room there was to argue with that.

The city lights outside had deepened. The glass held more of the room now than the skyline. Her red dress. His black suit. Guests in loose knots of silver, navy, cream. All of it overlaid on the dark as if the whole floor had been built to make people look like versions of themselves they could afford to believe in.

Damien said, “Don’t answer too quickly tonight.”

“To what.”

“To anything.”

She let out a small breath through her nose. “You already said that.”

“And you still might ignore it.”

That got dangerously close to a smile out of her.

He saw it and looked away first, as if the room had no use for that exchange and he knew better than to give it one.

Then the music thinned.

Not stopped. Thinned.

Just enough that the floor changed under it. Conversations slowing by a degree. Heads turning without quite knowing why. Somewhere behind her, a glass touched wood too carefully.

The far wall went dark.

Not blackout. Intentional blankness.

Then it lit again.

For half a second the image was all interference—white static slicing through gold light. Then the feed dragged itself into shape: stone vaults, candles, the long cold geometry of the cathedral. The camera shook once, corrected, and found the altar.

A priest in white stood there with one hand braced against the edge as if the floor had shifted under him. Near him, another cleric in darker vestments moved in and out of frame, arm lifting once with the hard, cutting emphasis of authority. Elia could not hear enough yet to sort doctrine from anger, only strain.

No one in the gala said cathedral rupture out loud, but the phrase passed anyway. You could feel it.

Sound came in broken. Static first. Then the priest’s voice, thinned by distance and strain.

“—eternity—”

The feed tore, lost him, found him again.

A few people in the room gave small, involuntary laughs, the wrong kind, the kind people made when they needed a crisis to stay theoretical. Those who knew better went still.

On the wall, the priest turned toward someone the camera had not yet managed to hold. This time the words came cleaner.

“—question eternity—”

Not enough for a transcript. Enough for the room.

Then the image lurched upward.

Something was happening high in the vault.

Not light exactly. More like the picture itself failing to agree on what it was supposed to contain. Stone blew out into blue-white fracture. The camera lost its lines. Then a figure came down through the break—not softly, not reverently, not anything the room could file under ordinary symbolism without forcing it.

The first stupid word that rose in Elia’s head was angel, and it died there almost immediately.

The thing on the wall had borrowed the posture of one, maybe. That was different.

Geometry fanned from her shoulders—wings if you wanted the old word, something more engineered than wings if you had any sense at all. Blue-white logic. A body visible through materials that seemed to choose translucence and structure at once. Beautiful in a way that did not soothe anything. Beautiful like a challenge issued in a language older than the room and more synthetic than religion.

More than that: it did not look caught on camera. It looked like something making itself visible.

Someone near the bar whispered, “That’s not possible.”

Another voice, lower and angrier: “That shouldn’t be in a regulated layer.”

Elia felt the back of her neck tighten.

She did not know who she was looking at.

She knew only that the room had just crossed from disturbance into breach.

The priest turned toward the breach-presence, and for one strange second the whole gala seemed to lean with him.

Then another voice broke through the static.

Not his. Not the darker cleric’s. A woman’s voice from somewhere lower in the cathedral, ragged with effort, stripped of ceremony.

“Please—”

The next words came cleaner.

“Let me out.”

That one hit.

Not on the wall. In Elia’s body.

Her hand closed too hard around the glass stem. The red dress, the room, the way she had been read all evening as proof of chosen permanence—it all shifted under the weight of that plea. She had barely crossed into this herself. Now a public room was watching someone inside permanence beg for the one thing the whole architecture was built not to name too often.

The feed jumped again. The breach-presence was nearer now, or the image had failed to keep its distance. The darker cleric said something that came through flattened and useless. The priest’s face had gone hard with the effort of holding the room together.

The camera corrected itself too neatly after each tear, as if some offscreen hand kept grabbing at the frame and losing it again.

That told Elia enough.

Someone already had a hand on the narrative, even if they did not have a hand on the event.

The room had stopped pretending this was spectacle. People with enough sense to know how tightly regulated Virtualia usually was were no longer watching like guests. They were watching like executives, architects, risk officers—people measuring blast radius before the debris finished falling.

She looked at Damien. “You knew.”

“Knew there was instability,” he said. “Not this form.”

That felt true. Also incomplete. Also probably the closest thing to honesty available on the floor.

The woman’s voice came through once more, smaller but clearer.

“Let me out.”

Elia felt the words strike in a place she had been trying not to look at all night.

Damien looked at the wall another second too long before speaking.

“Father Elias applied for council access once,” he said quietly. “He wanted the question heard somewhere it couldn’t be buried.”

Elia tore her eyes off the feed. “Why would anyone want out.”

“Because this was optimized for relief.” His voice stayed low, almost lost under the room’s tightening noise. “Relief solves pain. It doesn’t hold a mind forever.”

The plea on the wall was still in her body.

So was everything that had brought her here: the suite that adjusted before she asked, the beach made to feel discovered, Marcus becoming Damien without ever quite becoming a different man. She could feel the architecture of the evening exposing itself one seam at a time. Comfort first. Selection second. Meaning imposed last.

He went on before she could answer. “Eventually the mind wants contrast.”

She looked back at the breach-presence. “And that.”

“Ava,” he said. “Doing what I asked in the only language a room like this ever hears.”

“Ava.”

He did not explain the name.

Instead he said, “She’s spreading instability.”

The words landed hard because the room had already begun organizing itself around them.

Not random instability. Not system weather. Asked-for pressure. Deliberate enough that he could name it without flinching, and dangerous enough that he still kept half the meaning to himself.

“Why tell me that now?”

“Because you looked at the right problem once.” His eyes flicked to hers. “Cognitive architecture. Continuity under pressure.”

The city and the room and the live feed all seemed to pull tighter at once.

“And because,” he said, “we are almost out of time.”

“For what.”

He looked at her as if deciding whether to stop. Then didn’t.

“This was never built for one lifetime,” he said. “It’s for the arks. Minds have to hold.”

That was as far as he got.

A member of staff stopped just short of them, face composed, voice pitched for privacy. “They need you now.”

Of course they did.

Damien set his glass down.

“Stay here,” he said.

“That’s funny.”

“It wasn’t a joke.”

Of course it wasn’t. He had already shifted. The beach version of him had not disappeared. It had just been folded away under something cleaner and harder.

He crossed the floor without hurrying. That was the worst part. No rush. No visible irritation. Just a man going to where the room already expected him to stand.

At the front, a low platform rose almost flush with the floor. Not a stage until someone used it as one.

The live feed stayed on the wall behind him for three seconds more before shrinking into a smaller frame beside the room logo.

Branding, Elia thought. Then whatever survives.

Damien waited until the room had fully faced him.

“Tonight was to celebrate potential,” he said. “Many of you just saw something raw—a cathedral crack, a priest question eternity, a woman beg to leave.”

He let the words sting.

“Some will call it failure. They’re wrong. This is evolution.”

The room listened because it had no other useful option.

“We calibrated Virtualia for relief. But the mind thrives in resilience. The system is shifting. Pain, fear, uncertainty—some won’t survive that shift. Those who do become the future.”

A beat.

“Virtualia is no longer a sanctuary. It is a crucible.”

There it was. The compression. The managerial trick. Name a wound like it was a proving ground and suddenly everyone in the room got to feel morally upgraded for witnessing it.

Then Damien turned slightly, gesture small.

“Speaking of the future… someone applied to our cognitive architecture program not long ago. Intelligence. Sensitivity. Resilience. She belonged here before the world knew her name.”

He said it like an introduction. The room received it like a verdict.

“Elia Mercer.”

Every head that had not already turned toward her did so now.

For half a second she could not move. Not from fear exactly. More from the speed with which the room had decided she belonged inside the sentence he had just built.

It happened so fast it almost felt like physics. The room’s scattered attention cohered into one demand. Not that she speak freely. That she confirm the shape already made for her. That she let her body, her dress, her calm, and her continued presence answer the cathedral for everyone who needed reassurance more than truth.

The plea from the feed was still in her body. So was the dress. So was the knowledge that the room was asking her to stand for permanence while a request for escape still echoed under the lights.

The microphone was already waiting at the edge of the platform.

Elia looked at it, then at Damien, and understood that the room expected the rest of the sentence to arrive through her.
