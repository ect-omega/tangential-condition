Preference Mapping

The first two days went by in pieces.

Sleep. Waking. Somebody at the door with a tablet. A tray left on the table near the window. Another stretch of sleep she had not meant to take. The room changing around her in small ways that were too useful to call decorative.

On the first morning, she woke to a wall display already lit.

Welcome, Elia.
Continuity stable.
Orientation available when ready.

She stood there in the half-light with one hand still on the sheet, looking at her own name in company type.

Continuity stable.

The phrase sat there like paperwork trying to pass for comfort.

She touched the screen just to make it go away.

That was how most of the first day went. A line of text. Her hand reaching out to kill it. Another line appearing somewhere else a few minutes later as if the room had taken the gesture as data and adjusted.

A woman from acclimation came by before noon in pale gray and shoes too quiet on the floor.

"Good morning, Elia," she said, with the kind of voice people used when they wanted to cross a line without waking it. "I'm Mara. I'm here to help you settle."

Elia looked at the tablet in the woman's hand. "That's what this is called?"

Mara glanced down at the screen. "Orientation, support, settling. Depends which module you open."

"And which one am I in?"

Mara gave a small, trained smile that did not quite settle on her face. "The one that gets you through the first week."

The tablet held page after page of terms that all seemed designed to make the week smaller than it was.

Continuity support.
Social acclimation.
Embodiment stabilization.
Environmental preference mapping.

"What if I don't want any of it mapped yet," Elia asked.

Mara did not blink. "Some of it is already happening."

That was probably the most honest thing anyone said to her all day.

She signed almost nothing.

Most of it did not require signatures anyway.

The room learned her too quickly. That was what bothered her. Not the luxury of it. The responsiveness.

The lights rose softly instead of snapping on. The shower remembered the temperature she had set that morning and offered it back to her without asking. A blouse she had only touched in the closet got moved higher in the rotation the next time the doors opened. By the second morning the coffee came stronger and less sweet, as if the room had already decided she was the kind of person who drank bitterness on purpose.

She caught herself being grateful for some of it and hated that too.

It was hardest at the mirror.

Not because the reflection looked wrong. The opposite. That was the problem. She could stand at the sink and brush her teeth and look up and see a face that did not start an argument the second it met her eyes. She had expected more shock. More disbelief. Some recoil she could cling to if she needed proof that all of this had been done too fast and too cleanly and with too many people at the door.

Instead there was only the face, and then the aftereffect of the face.

The first morning she kept checking it sideways, as if turning too directly toward it might trigger something artificial. By the second morning she was forgetting to brace before she looked up. By the third, she caught herself leaning closer to the glass for no reason at all except that she still could not quite believe how much of her day had once been spent negotiating the first half-second of seeing herself.

That absence unsettled her more than any glamour ever could.

On the second day she opened one of the orientation windows just to see what would happen.

The system rendered a smiling guide figure into the corner of the room and began in the same soft tone every staff member seemed to share.

Permanent residency offers continuity across embodiment, environment, and long-span identity support--

She closed the window before the sentence finished.

A second later the wall gave her a new message instead.

Orientation paused.
Resume when ready.

She laughed once, sharp and tired, and walked away from it.

The room did not get offended. It just adjusted. A music channel came on somewhere deeper in the suite, low enough to miss unless she stopped moving. The curtains opened a little farther to let in more of the artificial afternoon. Somewhere behind a panel, air shifted.

Everything here acted like refusal was a preference setting.

By the middle of the second night she had started making little tests of her own.

She left the breakfast tray untouched until noon just to see if anyone came to check on her. No one did. Lunch arrived later and slightly lighter, as if the system had quietly concluded she was eating less in warm hours. She pulled three dresses from the closet and shoved them onto the bed in a wrinkled heap. The next time she opened the wardrobe, the hanging order had changed and two of the fabrics she hated most had disappeared from view.

Nothing here argued directly. It absorbed, rerouted, softened, learned.

She could never decide whether that was mercy or conquest.

At some point on the second evening, while standing barefoot in the middle of the room with a towel around her shoulders and one of the wall displays muttering silently to itself, she realized she had not heard anyone say Eli since the transfer floor.

That thought stopped her.

She stood very still for a moment and listened, as if the room might be saying the old name somewhere under its own noise.

It wasn't.

She said it once herself, under her breath, just to check the damage.

"Eli."

The name did not break. It did not sting. It just sat there in the room like something mailed to the wrong address.

She leaned one hand against the dresser and stared at the carpet until the wave passed.

Something had been taken from her. That was still true. The speed of it, the corridor, the forms, the way choice had narrowed until it was impossible to separate what she wanted from the machinery closing around it. None of that had stopped being true because the mirror had stopped hurting.

But another truth kept showing up too, quieter and meaner.

The body fit.

It fit when she crossed the room in the morning. It fit when she bent to tie the sash of a robe. It fit when she rolled onto one hip in bed and reached for the water glass in the dark. She did not have to narrate it to herself. Her muscles knew before language did.

That was the part she still had no decent place to put.

On the third morning Mara returned with a different tablet and the same careful face.

"How are you sleeping?" she asked.

"Like I got hit by a company."

Mara's expression changed by almost nothing. Maybe she had heard worse. Maybe she had heard that one before.

"Your recovery curve is normal," she said.

"My what."

"Resident sleep depth often overcorrects during the first forty-eight hours."

Elia stared at her. "You really can say anything if you make it sound clinical."

Mara looked at the screen. "It helps."

"Who."

That did it. A real pause. Small, but real.

Then Mara said, "Usually the person hearing it."

Elia looked away before the answer could do anything useful.

After Mara left, she stood in the middle of the room and took stock of everything she had begun accepting too quickly. The coffee. The light. The fabric. The body. The way the system kept using her name like it had always belonged to her and was waiting only for her to stop making a scene about it.

She was angry, she thought. She should still be more angry than this.

Instead what she mostly felt was tired. Tired, and then guilty about the tiredness, because fatigue made cooperation look too much like reason.

The wall opened another spread of suggestions around lunchtime.

Garden terrace.
Museum wing.
Shoreline leisure zone.
Low-stim social commons.
Meditative architecture path.

She read them without moving.

At the bottom of the interface a soft line of text appeared.

Outdoor environments recommended.

The room had noticed the way she kept standing by the window.

She almost closed the whole display on reflex. Then stopped.

The suite was beginning to feel less like a refuge than a hand resting flat against the small of her back. Helpful. Continuous. Impossible to ignore.

She needed out of it for a while, even if out only meant another managed environment with better weather.

The closet had already anticipated something.

When she opened it, a pale cover-up sat turned toward the front beside a swimsuit she did not remember selecting--only a quiet prompt she must have dismissed without thinking, logged and kept anyway.

She touched the cover-up first. Light. Cool. A little transparent in the sleeve.

The fabric looked expensive in the same way everything here did--like no one needed to ask how it would sit on her, because that had already been decided.

The old reflex flickered up--too much, too visible, not safe--and then died halfway to full force. Not gone. Just weaker. Not strong enough to make her put it back.

That unsettled her almost as much as the mirror had.

She dressed slowly.

At the last minute she nearly changed her mind and put on something more anonymous, more covered, something she could use to tell herself she was only going to look at the environment and not take part in it. Instead she stood in front of the mirror with one hand at her throat and watched herself hesitate.

No one else in the room. No one urging her one way or another. Just the body, the clothes, the bright coastal icon waiting on the wall, and the lingering fact that she wanted to know what the outside of this place looked like when it was pretending to be leisure.

That was enough.

She selected Shoreline leisure zone.

The air changed first.

Heat, then salt, then bright water.

When the beach resolved around her, she stood still for a moment with her feet in the sand and let the sun hit her shoulders.

It was ridiculous.

It was also, annoyingly, good.

A line of umbrellas ran pale across the shore. Music moved somewhere farther inland, low enough to be mistaken for wind if you were tired. Drinks glittered on small tables. Bodies crossed in easy patterns that looked casual until you watched long enough to notice how nothing ever quite got in anybody else's way.

Elia shaded her eyes and took one slow breath.

Then another.

The breeze moved through her hair in the way she had always imagined it should.

That landed harder than she wanted it to.

She started toward the water anyway.

Before she had gone ten steps, a server angled subtly out of someone else's path to leave the shoreline clear.

Even here, the environment was already making room for her.
