Automatic Continuation

When Eli finally logged out, his room felt meaner for having waited.

The overhead light was still off. His phone lay dark on the desk beside the laptop. Outside the window, the dorm courtyard had emptied down to one person crossing alone with a laundry bag over one shoulder. The screen reflected his face for a second before going black.

For a moment he stayed where he was, hands still on the keyboard.

Inside Virtualia, the adjustments had felt almost too small to matter. A change in the line of the jaw. The set of the shoulders. The way hair fell when he turned. A shift in balance more than shape. But those were the changes that kept undoing him, because none of them felt invented. They felt like corrections to a sentence he had been reading wrong for years.

He opened the profile menu again before he could stop himself.

Adaptive profile: active.

Beneath it, hidden two layers down in a field he only ever touched alone, was the name he had given the build weeks earlier and then refused to say aloud anywhere that counted.

Elia.

He stared at it until the letters started to lose their edges.

Not a fantasy name. Not a joke. Not a handle. It had come to him with the same quiet force as recognition, the feeling of finding something that had been waiting longer than he had language for. He did not think, in the privacy of that screen, that he was making her. He thought, with a kind of shame he could not explain to anyone, that he was finally leaving her less interrupted.

He shut the profile window and forced himself into bed.

Sleep came in scraps.

By morning there was an unread email from Virtualia sitting at the top of his inbox, clean subject line, no urgency marks, which somehow made it worse.

Adaptive Identity Continuity Update

He swiped past it.

That became the rhythm for the next several days. He woke. He buried the email under classes, bookstore shifts, safe replies to Jonathan. He told himself the refusal mattered. He told himself not opening something was still a choice.

It helped that the real world kept offering him enough small problems to work with.

The bursar hold took another day to clear. His advising portal returned a different error each time he tried it. One professor answered a question he had sent two weeks earlier with a reply so generic it might as well have been automated. The bookstore schedule changed twice more. Once would have been bad luck. Three times started to feel personal, except there was nothing to point at. No enemy. No event. Just the sense that everything took a little more effort than it should have.

None of it was big enough to say out loud without sounding fragile.

He tried to fix that with discipline.

He went to class early. Stayed late at work. Hit the gym until his arms shook, as if exhaustion might settle him back into the body the mirrors kept insisting belonged to him. He answered Jonathan's texts with clipped, neutral lines.

Yeah, I'm fine.
No, I didn't miss the deadline.
Yes, I'm thinking about the internship.

The lie in the last message sat in his chest long after he sent it.

By Thursday afternoon he had almost convinced himself the mood would pass if he starved it properly.

Then he opened the email.

The message was polite enough to be insulting.

Your adaptive profile remains active.
Recent engagement suggests sustained identity-continuity interest.
You may resume your transformation at any time.

At the bottom, in smaller type:

You do not need to decide all at once.

He read that line twice.

It sounded compassionate until he noticed what it assumed. That a decision was already underway. That whatever he was doing counted, even now, as part of a process with a direction.

He closed the email without deleting it.

An hour later Jonathan called.

Eli let it ring once, twice, then answered because not answering would turn the silence into its own conversation.

"You busy?" Jonathan asked.

"Walking back from class."

"You sound tired."

"Everybody says that like it's new information."

A pause. Street noise moved between them. Eli cut across the quad, backpack riding awkwardly on one shoulder, students fanning around him in loose clusters. He could already hear Jonathan deciding how careful to be.

"I've been thinking about the other night," Jonathan said.

"That makes one of us."

"That isn't true."

"No?"

Jonathan exhaled. "I came on too hard."

There it was. Not apology exactly. Revision.

Eli stepped around a spill of bikes chained outside the library. "You tracked my sessions."

"I checked a dashboard I still had access to."

"That's not better."

"I didn't say it was." Another pause. "I said I came on too hard."

Eli pushed through the library doors and into the lobby, where the air smelled faintly of paper, wet coats, and overheated wiring. "What do you want me to do with that?"

"I want you to hear the rest."

He almost said no. Instead he moved to the side wall and leaned there while students passed in and out around him.

Jonathan's voice lowered. "I'm not trying to humiliate you."

"I know."

"I'm not trying to make you smaller."

Eli closed his eyes.

That landed worse than anger would have. He could hear the care in it. That was the problem. It made resentment feel ungrateful, made his own interior feel like something he had to apologize for before he had even named it.

Jonathan went on. "I'm trying to keep you from drifting into something that doesn't give people back."

Eli looked through the glass doors at the campus lawn beyond them. "You don't want me protected."

"What?"

"You want me recognizable."

Jonathan was quiet for long enough that Eli could picture him in his office, hand pressed flat to the desk, trying not to let the conversation outrun his control.

"That isn't fair," he said finally.

"It's true."

"No. It's incomplete." Jonathan's voice stayed calm, which only made the line cut cleaner. "You think I'm trying to force you into a version of yourself that makes me comfortable. I'm trying to make sure there's still a version of yourself left once these systems finish with you."

Eli almost laughed.

There was the difference between them. Jonathan still believed the threat arrived from outside, all at once, after a person had enough time to make some reasonable choices. He did not understand that for Eli the erosion was already here, already ambient. It lived in the effort of crossing a room correctly, in the drag of introductions, in the way mirrors sometimes felt like official documents instead of reflections.

"I'll think about it," Eli said, because anything truer would start a fight he did not have the strength for.

Jonathan's relief came too quickly. "That's all I'm asking."

After the call ended, Eli stood in the library lobby until the phone screen went black in his hand. He could still feel the outline of Jonathan's voice pressing against him, gentle as a hand at the back of the neck.

That night he did not log in.

He made himself eat vending-machine soup with one of the guys from work, laughed in the right places, skimmed two readings badly, and went to sleep with the laptop shut.

At 2:14 a.m. he woke with the feeling that he had forgotten something important. The room was silent. His phone was face down. Nothing had happened.

Still, he reached for the laptop.

Virtualia opened without protest.

The street he returned to was the same one from before, rain-dark and mostly empty, windows lit in warm rectangles along the block. He had meant to stay five minutes. Instead he wandered for nearly an hour, saying nothing, changing almost nothing, just existing there in the build he had been teaching himself not to call by name too often.

When he passed a reflective panel this time, he did not go straight to it. He kept walking, then circled back as if the second approach would make the act less revealing.

The profile had saved the last adjustments. Softer cheek line. Mouth different at rest. A body still recognizable as his and somehow no longer fighting itself the same way.

He moved one step to the left.

She moved with him.

He stopped.

That was the part he could not explain. Not the feminine shape, not the alterations, not the fact of wanting. The unbearable part was how little translation it seemed to require. Outside, every room demanded decisions about emphasis, posture, what to hide, how much to overcorrect, when to let a joke stand because fixing it would cost too much. In here, the energy those calculations consumed just... stayed available.

He opened the name field again and this time did not look away from it.

Elia.

He said it once, quietly, to the empty room.

Then he shut the audio channel as if even Virtualia had no right to hear him do it.

Friday a friend from class dragged him to a house party two blocks off campus after Eli said no twice and then failed to think of a third version with enough conviction in it.

The house smelled like beer, citrus cleaner, and too many bodies sharing stale air. Music hit the floorboards hard enough to make the cups on the kitchen counter tremble. Someone he knew only by first name shoved a drink into his hand and disappeared before Eli could refuse it.

He stood near the sink and tried to look occupied.

The room around him kept performing ease. People laughing too loud. One couple arguing in the hallway with smiling mouths and dead eyes. Somebody trying to start a dance circle in a living room too small to allow one. It all felt like a scene people had agreed to keep alive through mutual embarrassment.

In the dark window above the sink, he caught his reflection between two strangers.

Sharp jaw. Broad shoulders. Arms folded too close to the body, as if even at rest he was bracing. He looked like a version of himself that had been signed off on by committee.

He left before midnight without telling anyone.

Back in his room, he sat on the edge of the bed fully clothed and stared at the laptop until the screen timed itself to sleep.

Then his phone buzzed.

Dad: Stay grounded.
Dad: Don't lose yourself in something built to flatter confusion.

Eli laughed once, bitter enough to hurt, and opened Virtualia before the sound was fully out of him.

Welcome back, Eli.
Would you like to resume your transformation?

He clicked yes before he could pretend to weigh it.

The avatar loaded with the same altered geometry, but tonight he pushed further. A little more length in the face. A different fall to the waist. A voice sample he had saved and never activated. The changes were small enough to be deniable and large enough to make his pulse climb.

He walked her through a quieter district beyond the market lanes. Neon washed the street in lavender and blue. A group of strangers passed laughing, and one of them nodded at him without hesitation, the glance landing exactly where it should. No pause. No recalculation.

That almost broke him.

He did not want admiration. He did not even want fantasy, not really. He wanted the absence of drag. The absence of that split-second audit people made, conscious or not, before deciding what category they had placed him in. Here the category felt solved in advance, and solved in a direction that did not make him wince.

When he logged out close to dawn, the room felt narrower than it had four hours earlier.

Saturday he tried to punish himself back into proportion.

He deleted the desktop shortcut and told himself that counted as something. He took an extra shift at the bookstore when his manager asked, though the ask came with the dazed apology of someone reading off a schedule she did not remember making. He skipped lunch and spent the break outside in the cold with a coffee gone bitter in ten minutes. Later he went to the gym again and forced himself through a full circuit while mirrors multiplied him from bad angles.

None of it changed the fact that his body felt most natural only when he was no longer fully inhabiting the version of it everyone else could see.

By evening the Virtualia email had been replaced by something more specific.

Adaptive profile progression detected.
Review continuity options.

He did not click it.

Instead he opened the login window in sandbox mode.

That became the compromise he built for himself. Sandbox only. No full environment loops. No long sessions. He would log in long enough to change one detail, then leave before the prompts could gather force. A line of the mouth. The height of the heel. A slower gait. He told himself this kept the boundary intact.

It did not.

Every exit produced new language.

Preference data retained.
Identity drift confirmed.
Continuation profile updated.

He switched devices as if the system might respect the symbolism of a different screen. It didn't. The same prompts met him on his tablet, his laptop, the phone app he had forgotten was still installed.

You do not need to decide all at once.
Partial engagement accepted.
We can continue from where you stop.

He began to understand the trap not as force, but as accounting. The system did not need a clean yes if it could turn every maybe into stored momentum.

He tested that theory by refusing it harder.

For one full day he did not log in at all. He answered emails. Went to class. Took notes he would not remember later. Laughed at a joke in the bookstore break room about people who spent so long in immersive worlds they came back speaking like customer-service bots. The room laughed with the easy meanness of people sure the target was elsewhere.

Eli looked down at his sandwich and said nothing.

That night he dreamed in interface text.

By Monday the real world had begun to feel less hostile than poorly synchronized. Nothing dramatic enough to point at. Just mismatches. A shirt that sat wrong across the shoulders. A professor glancing at him twice before calling on someone else. The bookstore scanner lagging a half second too long. His own name in an attendance system reading as both official and slightly inaccurate, like a legal term for something he had already started outgrowing.

He skipped one class to sit in the library stacks where nobody would ask him to define anything and opened the Virtualia profile menu on his phone.

Adaptive profile: Elia.
Continuity state: active.

He looked at the screen until his vision blurred.

The name did not feel stolen. It did not feel performative. It felt private in the way a bruise was private, real before visible.

He closed the menu and shoved the phone into his pocket so hard it almost slipped through the lining.

By Tuesday evening, the next email arrived.

Virtualia HQ: Final Confirmation Required - Your Permanent Program Is Ready

He read the subject line standing in the bookstore stockroom with a cart of returned texts in front of him and the flat institutional smell of cardboard all around.

He opened it.

Verify within 48 hours.
Failure to respond will result in automatic continuation of your residence profile.

At the bottom:

You may confirm now, later, or through partial progression.
Your continuity work will be preserved.

He read the phrase automatic continuation three times.

It no longer sounded like a threat. It sounded like policy.

That was worse.

He closed the email and finished shelving the cart because there was nothing else to do with his hands.

Later, in his room, he tried to turn indecision into a hiding place.

He stayed in sandbox mode. Logged in just long enough to smooth a face, alter a stride, then cut the session before any confirmation prompt could settle. He let timers expire. Closed windows without answering them. He switched from phone to tablet to laptop as if one of the machines might suddenly start believing in symbolic resistance.

It didn't matter.

Every refusal came back translated.

Preference data retained.
Identity drift confirmed.
Continuation profile updated.

He shut the laptop, paced, opened it again. Sat on the floor with his back against the bed frame and watched the countdown in the corner of the email window drop by one hour.

47 hours.

He stood up, crossed to the desk, and unplugged the laptop like that would somehow interrupt the logic behind it. The battery took over without a flicker.

You do not need to decide all at once.
Partial engagement accepted.

He laughed then, once, because the wording was so patient it bordered on intimate. The system had anticipated delay. Anticipated shame. Anticipated the small dramatic gestures he might use to keep calling himself undecided.

That was what broke something open in him. Even hesitation had been priced in. The pauses, the half-logins, the exits he kept mistaking for autonomy were not outside the process. They were part of intake.

He sat back down.

On the screen, Elia's profile waited in the corner menu, nothing coercive about it at all. Just the name. Just the shape. Just the version of himself that had started feeling less like an experiment than the only room in which he could stop flinching.

Outside the window, someone down in the courtyard shouted a name he didn't recognize. A bike chain scraped metal. Somewhere farther off, a siren moved through the city and kept going.

Eli looked at the countdown again.

43 hours left.
