Marcus Interface

The first two days passed in a blur of sleep, screens, and staff who acted like none of this needed much discussion now that it had already happened.

A woman from acclimation came by twice a day with a tablet and a voice pitched so carefully it never quite sounded human. Continuity windows. Support tiers. Social onboarding. Cognitive settling. Every phrase made the last week sound administrative instead of violent.

Elia signed almost nothing.

Most of it didn't require signatures anyway.

The room learned her fast. Light came up slowly in the morning. Breakfast changed when she skipped too much of it. The closet suggested things that fit too well. Coffee arrived stronger on the days she slept badly. The care was so exact it made her suspicious of her own relief.

By the third morning she had stopped flinching at mirrors.

That didn't mean she was used to them. Only that the first shock had burned off and left something steadier behind. She could brush her teeth, look up, and see her own face in the glass without that first jolt of disbelief. The face there was hers. That part had settled hard and fast.

Everything else lagged.

The way staff said resident. The chime when some new access tier opened. The fact that nobody asked whether she wanted any of this described in past tense or present tense. Around here the argument was over. Whatever the week had been, the system was already treating it like a settled thing.

By late afternoon she was too restless to stay in the room.

The wall opened a fan of suggested environments. Garden terrace. Museum wing. Shoreline leisure zone. She almost picked the museum just to be difficult, then chose the beach because she wanted air, even fake air, more than she wanted to make a point.

The shift came over her in a wash. Heat first. Then salt. Then bright water and pale umbrellas and sand clean enough to look decorative until she dug her toes into it and felt grit.

She stood there for a minute with one hand shading her eyes.

It was ridiculous. Of course it was. A beach inside the same system that had rushed her into intake and wrapped the whole thing in calm language after the fact. Still, the sun felt good on her shoulders. The breeze moved through her hair the way she had always wanted it to. She could feel herself relaxing and hated how little that hatred changed.

She found an empty chair near the water and sat down. The cover-up the closet had suggested that morning was still on her, thin enough to let the heat through. Out on the horizon, something white and sleek cut across the water too smoothly to be a normal boat.

"Any chance this one's free?"

She looked up.

The man standing by the next chair had sunglasses in one hand and a drink in the other. Easy face. Clean shirt. Good posture without looking stiff about it. The sort of man who probably never had to work too hard to make a room receive him well.

"You're asking after you've already stopped," she said.

He smiled. "You caught me."

"You can still keep walking."

"I could." He looked at the empty chair. "But you look like you'd hate another hour by yourself."

That got her attention in spite of herself.

He sat when she didn't tell him not to.

"Marcus," he said.

"Elia."

He repeated her name back without the tiny pause everybody else still had the first time. "Good to meet you."

She watched him for a second. "You didn't do the thing."

"What thing?"

"The extra beat." She glanced out at the water. "Like you need a second to decide whether the name belongs where I put it."

Marcus followed her gaze. "You're not in a place where that's supposed to be difficult."

"That wasn't an answer."

"No," he said. "It wasn't."

A server walked past. Marcus lifted two fingers without looking up. The server nodded once and kept moving.

Elia noticed.

"So you know people," she said.

"Enough to avoid bad drinks."

"That sounds practiced."

"That's because it works."

A glass appeared a minute later on the table beside her.

"I didn't order anything," she said.

The server gave her a polite glance. "You don't have to."

Then they were gone.

Elia looked at the drink, then at Marcus. "That normal?"

"For here?" He took a sip of his own. "Pretty much."

The drink was cold enough to bite. Something citrus, something bitter at the back. She hated that it was exactly what she wanted.

Marcus watched her notice that and didn't comment.

They sat in silence for a little while. Not uncomfortable silence. Just two people watching the water and pretending the place didn't have hidden settings somewhere breathing behind the walls.

Finally he said, "How's the body?"

She answered too fast. "Fine."

He didn't push.

She looked down at her own hands. The nails were neat. The wrist still surprised her at the wrong angle. "Better than fine."

Marcus nodded once. "Yeah."

That was all.

No soft speech about becoming yourself. No pitch. Just that one small sound of recognition, which made it harder to sort him into the usual categories.

She asked, "Do you actually like it here?"

"Some parts."

"That sounds like no."

"That sounds like I've been here long enough to get selective."

"You live in Virtualia full-time?"

He leaned back in the chair. "Most of the time."

That answer sat oddly with him. Not wrong. Just incomplete in a way she noticed without understanding.

The conversation stayed light after that in the way light talk sometimes doesn't. Music. Bad food in public zones. What kind of shoes got ruined by sand no matter how expensive they were. How every beautiful place in here started feeling stranger the minute you noticed how much it had been adjusted for you. He was funny in a dry, sideways way. A little too observant. Mildly flirtatious without making a show of it. Enough to be plausible. Enough to make the extra attention around him feel, for a while, like the sort of thing that might happen around anyone attractive and comfortable in their own skin.

But the oddness never fully went away.

A woman passing with a towel over one shoulder looked once at Marcus and then again at Elia. A server adjusted an umbrella farther down the row and checked back toward them after. Nothing dramatic. Nothing she could have pointed to without sounding thin-skinned. Still, the place kept making room around them in small ways.

By the time the sun had started going gold at the edges, Marcus stood and stretched.

"There's a bar inland that does less beach," he said.

"That's a terrible pitch."

"It works on the right people."

"And I'm the right people?"

"You've looked one minute away from starting a fight with the scenery since I sat down."

She laughed before she could stop herself. "That isn't fair."

"It's a little fair."

There was no good reason to go anywhere with a man she didn't know in a system that had already demonstrated exactly how much steering it thought she required. There was also no good reason to stay there and let the beach keep working on her.

"What kind of bar?" she asked.

"The kind with darker lighting and worse music than it thinks it has."

"That's almost a real answer."

"It's enough to get you moving."

That was how she ended up walking beside him off the sand.

The shift inland happened gradually. Boardwalk first. Then shops. Then a strip of bars and restaurants close enough to the water to keep the salt in the air. Marcus moved through the crowd as if the place had already made a little space for him.

At first she thought she was imagining it. The hostess glancing up and straightening before he even reached the door. A server at another place turning, seeing him, and changing direction. By the time they got to the tiki bar it was too patterned to ignore.

"Nobody actually has to ask whether you belong somewhere, do they," she said.

Marcus held the door open for her. "That line was better on the boardwalk."

The bar was cooler than the beach and darker than outside called for. Fans overhead. Hanging lights. A back wall of bottles too pretty to trust. By the time she took it in, somebody was already leading them to a corner table.

Marcus didn't touch the menu.

Elia took hers just to make a point.

The waitress came over with a smile that was almost casual. "Good to see you."

"You too," Marcus said.

She looked to Elia. "Something sharp? Or are you in the mood to be disappointed properly?"

Elia looked up. "That was almost helpful."

The waitress smiled once, really this time. "Sharp, then."

Marcus looked amused.

"That happens a lot?" Elia asked after the waitress left.

"What?"

"People knowing how to handle you."

"More than is probably healthy."

"That answer is terrible."

"I know."

Her drink came fast. Too fast, maybe. She took a sip and was annoyed to find it was exactly right again.

Marcus didn't comment.

The room around them filled up without getting much louder. A couple at the rail. Two men in jackets talking business three tables over. Somebody feeding coins into a jukebox interface that clearly didn't need coins. Less openly managed than the beach, maybe, but only by degree. There were still patterns if she watched for them. Hosts redirecting people without seeming to. Staff keeping the corner around their booth from getting too crowded.

"So," Elia said, "do you actually work here, or do you just behave like a man who's tipped enough people over a long period of time?"

"A little of both."

"That wasn't the question."

"No. But it's a better answer than the real one."

She looked at him. "You know more than you're saying."

"Almost certainly."

"And that doesn't bother you."

"It bothers people all the time."

That irritated her, mostly because it was the kind of line she should have hated and didn't, not fully, coming from him.

A host crossed the room, bent toward Marcus, said something too low for her to hear, then moved off again.

There it was.

Elia set her glass down. "Okay."

Marcus looked up. "Okay what?"

"The part under the part."

"That's vague."

"It's enough."

For the first time since the beach, he looked like he might have to give her a straighter answer than he wanted to.

"There's a private floor upstairs," he said.

"Of course there is."

He almost smiled. "That was always going to be your response."

"Then maybe don't hand me the line so cleanly."

"That wasn't the intention."

"No?"

"No." He held her gaze. "Not entirely."

She sat back and folded her arms. "That's not reassuring."

"I'm not trying to reassure you." He said it without edge. "I'm trying not to insult you."

That landed harder than she expected.

"Who's upstairs?" she asked.

He looked at her for a long enough second that she could almost feel the day turning.

Then he said, "Before you go any farther with me, there's something I should stop making convenient."

Her chest tightened a little.

"That sounds bad."

"It depends how much you enjoy omission."

She waited.

Marcus set down his glass. The easy tilt in his posture didn't vanish. It just tightened. Less beach. Less joke. The same face, suddenly using itself differently.

"Marcus is easier down here," he said. "Upstairs they expect Damien."

For a second she didn't understand the sentence.

Then she did.

It wasn't that he looked different all at once. That would have been easier. It was worse than that. The face stayed the same. The voice stayed close enough to itself. What changed was the arrangement. The amount of room he seemed to occupy without moving.

She stared at him. "You mean Damien Voss."

"Yes."

She looked away from him, then back. The whole afternoon shifted in her head. Not fake. Not some ridiculous performance from first line to last. The jokes still felt like jokes. The flirtation still felt real enough to be dangerous. But there had been more structure under it than she'd wanted to believe.

You could have said that on the beach.

"I could have." He didn't bother defending it. "You would have walked."

"Maybe."

"Yes," he said. "Probably."

She sat with that.

The waitress brought the check without either of them asking. Damien, she still couldn't think the other name without the rest of it now, signed without looking.

"Was any of it yours?" she asked.

The question didn't seem to surprise him.

"Yes."

"Which part?"

"The part I didn't hand off."

That was too close to an answer. She hated that.

He put the pen down. "You've been getting noticed since you arrived."

"By who?"

"A room," he said. "Several, probably." Then, after half a beat, "I prefer first looks before consensus settles in."

That was the first answer all day that sounded like him in full.

Not warm. Not open either. Just cleanly incomplete.

"So I'm a curiosity."

"At minimum."

"That's an ugly thing to say."

"It would be uglier to dress it up."

She stood. So did he.

They left through a side corridor instead of the main entrance. The music dropped away behind them. The air changed. Less salt. More cooled stone and filtered air.

Halfway down the hall an attendant stepped out with a tray of cosmetics and a small steamer.

Elia stopped.

Damien stopped too. "Optional."

"That's a suspicious word in this place."

"I know."

The attendant didn't smile. "Private floor upstairs. Some people prefer a quick touch-up before they walk in."

Elia looked down at herself. Wind in her hair. Cover-up tied badly at the waist. Smudged liner at one eye. She hated that she was noticing it now that it had been offered back to her as a choice.

Damien said, "You can walk in exactly like this if you want."

"That sounds like another test."

"It sounds like a room upstairs."

She said nothing to that.

"Do you get this done too?" she asked.

"Worse."

That got a small, unwilling laugh out of her. She let the attendants fix only what she could not stop being aware of once it had been pointed out.

They were quick. Powder. A cleaner mouth line. Fingers at the clasp of one shoulder. Less makeover than adjustment. Damien waited with his hands in his pockets, looking down the corridor instead of at her.

When they moved on, the hall narrowed. The light changed underfoot. Voices ahead now. Low, layered, expensive.

Her pulse started climbing.

Damien noticed.

"You should know one thing before you walk in," he said.

"That would have been useful six hours ago."

"Probably."

She folded her arms. "Go ahead, then."

He slowed near the last turn. "Rooms like this decide too quickly." His glance went ahead, not at her. "They'll do it to you in under a minute if you let them."

"And if I don't let them?"

He looked at her then. "Then they have to work harder."

That was all.

No explanation of why she mattered. No clean admission of what he planned to do with her once she was inside. Just that one warning, which somehow told her more than a longer answer would have.

She asked, "Why bring me at all?"

He took a second with that one. "Because being talked about from a distance is usually worse."

Still withholding. Still true enough to be useful.

"What happens if I walk out?" she asked.

"You walk out."

"And then?"

"Then you've walked out."

He said it evenly enough that she almost believed him, which was probably part of the problem.

He stepped aside and left the opening clear.

For a second she caught herself in the dark panel beside the door. Not the late-night version from reflection screens and shelter glass. Not unfinished. A woman dressed for a room she hadn't agreed to enter until now, still irritated, still curious, still carrying the aftertaste of choices she hadn't made cleanly.

Damien touched the wall panel.

The doors opened.

Warm light spilled out. Cooler air hit her skin a second later, carrying citrus, expensive perfume, and the clean bite of ice from the bar. Somewhere deeper in the room, glass knocked softly against glass.

"Ready?" he asked.

"No," Elia said.

Then she walked in anyway.

The room was full without being crowded. Small groups near the windows. Others by the bar. Everyone with enough space around them to feel important without having to ask for it. Everything expensive and controlled enough to look casual only if you had never been poor.

The second thing she noticed was what happened to Damien when the room saw him.

Nothing theatrical. Nobody announced anything. But lines of attention shifted. Conversations bent. Staff adjusted themselves a fraction faster. The version of him she had spent the afternoon with was still there. He just fit differently here.

He leaned slightly toward her. "Stay close until you get your footing."

It wasn't warm. It wasn't cold either. Just useful.

As they moved in, she could feel eyes landing and moving on and then coming back. Not on him alone now. On the pair of them. On what the pairing might mean. On the story starting to write itself before anyone had said a word.

Damien took a drink from a passing tray and handed it to her without asking what she wanted. The gesture was so practiced it almost disappeared.

"Is this where I'm supposed to look grateful?" she asked.

"If you do, they'll read it as permission."

She held the glass without drinking.

Someone across the room looked over too quickly. Two men near the windows lowered their voices when they saw who Damien had brought in. At the edge of the bar, someone smiled at her with the confidence of a person who thought they already understood what she was.
