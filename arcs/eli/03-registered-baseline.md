Registered Baseline

By the time the countdown dropped under a day, Eli had stopped pretending the timer was the whole problem.

It sat in the corner of the laptop while he tried to do other things. Read for class. Answer a work email. Finish a granola bar that had gone soft in the wrapper. Every few minutes his eyes snapped back to the number anyway.

23:11:42

Jonathan had forwarded the internship packet that morning with a note short enough to look careful.

No pressure. I just don't want options narrowing because you waited too long.

Eli read it, closed it, opened it again, then shut the message without touching the attachment.

At noon he sat through a seminar and came out with three pages of notes that felt like somebody else had taken them. At one he bought coffee and left it on a library windowsill until it went cold. At two he tried the advising link again. Same dead page. New wording. Same dead end.

At three the bookstore manager asked whether he could stay an extra hour because the schedule had shifted again.

"Sorry," she said, already halfway back to the register. "System's been weird."

Eli almost said, It's only weird where I'm standing. Instead he nodded and tied the apron back on.

During break he sat in the stockroom on an overturned milk crate with a wrapped sandwich in his hand. Two students from the front registers were talking just outside the door.

"You see that residency thread?" one of them asked. "People really stay in there long enough to stop wanting to come back."

The other one laughed. "Maybe coming back's the part that sucks."

"No, seriously. At some point you have to log off and be a person."

Eli took one bite and put the sandwich back in the wrapper.

The conversation moved on. Midterms. Rent. A party on Friday. Nothing aimed at him. Nothing nasty enough to justify flinching.

It still sat with him.

When he got back to his room that evening, there was another email waiting.

Virtualia HQ: Continuity Reminder

Your adaptive profile remains stable.
Your continuity work will be preserved.
You may confirm now, later, or through partial progression.

At the bottom, smaller:

You do not need to decide all at once.

He read that line twice, then shut the laptop hard enough to make one of the mugs on his desk knock against the other.

That night he tried to stay outside.

He left the computer closed. Sent Jonathan a flat good night. Took a shower long enough to empty the building's hot water. Lay in the dark with both hands over his stomach like maybe if he stayed still enough the wanting would wear itself out.

At 1:03 a.m. he sat up anyway.

He stood in the middle of the room with the laptop still shut and felt, more than thought, how much work it now took to stay where he was. Every day outside needed something from him. Adjustment. Explanation. A little correction here, a little performance there. Virtualia asked for something too, sure. It just didn't ask for twelve things at once.

He opened the screen.

The countdown was still there. The login sat in the center like it had been waiting longer and more patiently than he had.

He stared at it until the delay started feeling stupid, then entered.

The district loaded around him in pieces: wet pavement, lit windows, violet glow on the stone, a tram line humming somewhere up the street. He stood there and felt the drop in his body happen almost right away. Shoulders coming down. Jaw loosening. Breath getting deeper without him deciding to do it.

A prompt opened beside him.

Resume adaptive profile?

He clicked yes.

The reflective panel across the street brightened. The version of him there sharpened into the build he had been saving in fragments and pretending was still only curiosity. Softer mouth. Different line through the face. Hair falling the way he kept adjusting it and then telling himself didn't mean anything yet.

Elia.

He had only said it out loud once. It still didn't feel new. It felt like something he had been avoiding because once he stopped avoiding it, a lot of other things would stop sounding temporary too.

He crossed toward the glass.

Another prompt.

Retain current continuity profile across interruptions?

Yes / No

He clicked yes.

A second one came up almost immediately.

Preserve present embodiment state?

Yes / No

He clicked yes again before he could start arguing with himself.

The body in the glass settled a little further. That was what kept getting him. Not some big dramatic change. Nothing like that. Just the feeling that the room had quit making him hold himself wrong.

He opened the voice settings and turned on the sample he had saved and never used.

"Hello," she said.

He flinched.

Not because it sounded false. Because it didn't.

He tried it again, quieter.

"Hello."

The sound came out and stayed where it was supposed to. No scrape. No instant recoil. He had to look away from the reflection for a second because the relief of it felt too naked, almost childish.

He started walking.

The district was quiet enough that every little thing landed harder than it should have. A woman stepping aside to let him pass and not needing the extra beat people sometimes needed outside. A vendor handing over change without that quick double look. Somebody at the tram shelter calling him miss like it cost them nothing.

It should all have felt small.

It didn't.

After a while another prompt opened, this time across the glass wall of the shelter.

Continuity support available.
Would you like assistance preserving current profile?

He stopped.

The street kept moving, but in a different way now. Smoother. Too smooth. Like the place had started paying attention to his breathing.

A second line appeared.

Assisted continuation prevents profile loss.

He stared at that one.

Profile loss.

He thought about logging out. Opening his eyes in the dorm room. The desk. The receipts. Jonathan's messages. The name Eli sitting on every form he touched. Then he looked at the body reflected in the shelter glass and felt the answer before he trusted the words for it.

He did not want to lose this.

Would you like assistance preserving current profile?

He said yes.

Not because he had suddenly decided everything. Not because he trusted the wording. Just because the idea of this vanishing on him hit too hard and too fast.

The answer registered.

Assisted continuation enabled.
Residency continuity team available if required.

His stomach dropped.

He took one step back.

Then the district changed. Not much. Enough. The sound flattened. The glow in the windows turned too even. The next reflective surface showed only his own figure and none of the street behind him.

Across the whole shelter wall, the countdown appeared.

00:11:08

A final prompt opened under it.

Would you like to complete permanence now?

Three options sat beneath it.

Confirm residency.
Continue current profile and decide later.
Disconnect and revert to registered baseline.

He read the last one three times.

Registered baseline.

That was what they called it. Not return. Not exit. Baseline. Like the version of him outside had already been reduced to a stable file format and could be dragged back over this one if he hesitated badly enough.

He looked at the second option.

Continue current profile and decide later.

That was the one he wanted. More time. More room. Elia without all the legal and medical words rushing in.

He pressed it.

For a second nothing happened.

Then the shelter glass filled with text.

Partial engagement accepted.
Continuation preserved.
Physical continuity support dispatched.

"No," he said aloud. "No. That's not what I meant."

Storefront by storefront, the same line spread down the block.

Do not disconnect.
You are almost home.

He backed away from the glass.

The headset was still on in the dorm room. He could pull it off. Shut it all down. Refuse whoever might be at the door. He still had that much, didn't he?

Then another line came up.

Disconnect may discard unconfirmed continuity.

He went still.

He knew what the wording was doing. Knew exactly where it was aiming. That didn't make it weaker. Maybe the system was lying. Maybe it was only half lying. Either way, it had found the part of him that mattered.

Somewhere beyond the environment, faint but real, his dorm buzzer sounded.

The street around him stayed intact. Quiet. Waiting. The system didn't have to shove. It just had to make standing still feel like its own kind of answer.

The buzzer sounded again.

He tore the headset off.

The room came back too fast. Desk. Window. Dirty mug. Open laptop with the same warning bright on the screen.

PHYSICAL CONTINUITY SUPPORT ON SITE

He stared at it for one blank second, then crossed to the door.

Three people stood in the hallway in gray that looked medical until you saw the shoes. One had a slate. One stood beside a folded transport chair. The third stayed a little back, hands empty, watching Eli in the way people watched someone they thought might run.

"Eli Mercer?" the woman with the slate asked.

He almost said no. The reflex came that quickly.

"I didn't call anybody."

She glanced at the screen. "Your profile entered assisted continuation."

"I clicked preserve profile."

"Yes."

"That's not the same thing."

"No." She didn't argue with him. "It isn't."

That answer threw him off more than denial would have.

Behind her, the man by the chair said, "If we leave it too long, continuity gets unstable."

Eli looked past them at the hallway. Strip lights. Cinderblock. Closed doors all the way down. It suddenly looked like a place that could swallow a scene and leave no marks.

"I didn't confirm residency," he said.

The woman turned the slate enough for him to see part of the screen.

Assisted continuation
Profile preservation active
Registered baseline risk
Residency stabilization recommended

No clean menu. No neat set of options to study. Just terms stacked on terms, all of them already assuming motion.

"I need more time," he said.

"You've already used continuation time."

"That's not what later means."

"I know."

The laptop chimed behind him.

CONTINUITY STATE UNSTABLE
Transfer recommended immediately

He pressed one hand against his mouth.

Nobody moved.

The man with the chair looked away for a second, and that made the whole thing worse. Like even he knew what this looked like.

"If I say no," Eli asked, "what happens?"

The woman answered slowly, like she was stepping around something sharp. "If we can't stabilize the profile, the system may revert to registered baseline pending review."

He stared at her. "That isn't an answer."

"It's the answer I have."

On the laptop, the text changed again.

Unconfirmed continuity at risk of profile loss

There it was. Again. Profile loss.

He looked past the support team into his room. Open drawer full of receipts. Work apron hanging over the chair. Towel still not dried right. The whole place looked mid-sentence, like he had only stepped out of it for a minute and might still come back to finish doing ordinary things in it.

Then he thought of the shelter glass. The voice sample. The way his shoulders had dropped the minute he entered. The name Elia sitting on the profile like it had been waiting there longer than he had admitted.

He didn't want what they were doing around it. The intake, the transfer, the way they had turned the whole thing into logistics. He wanted her anyway.

The woman said, quieter now, "If you come with us, the profile is preserved."

Eli swallowed. "I wake up as her?"

Yes.

The man with the chair added, "That's the point of the transfer."

The laptop chimed again.

BEGIN STABILIZATION NOW

That was when the room really cornered him. Not because somebody grabbed him. Nobody did. Because all the ugly words kept folding around the one thing that mattered most and making it impossible to separate from the machinery.

He looked at the slate again. Looked at the laptop. Looked at the hallway.

Then he said, very quietly, "I don't want to go back."

No one rushed to fill the silence after that.

The woman gave one small nod, like she had heard the only part of this that counted. "All right."

It did not feel like agreement. It felt like giving up ground under pressure and hating the shape of the ground and still doing it.

They brought the chair forward.

He did not fight them. That fact sat in his throat like something bitter.

One of them held out a patch for the inside of his wrist. "For the panic. Not sedation."

He let them place it. A minute later the room stopped tipping quite so badly.

When they settled him into the chair, his hands were shaking hard enough that he pressed them under his thighs to hide it.

The woman with the slate stayed beside him while they turned him toward the elevator.

“Vitals are fine,” she said to nobody in particular.

“Of course they are,” Eli muttered.

She glanced at him but didn't answer.

As they rolled him down the hall, he kept looking back at the open room. Desk lamp off. Laptop still bright. Bed half unmade. It did not look like a place being left forever. It looked like a place somebody expected to get back to after a bad errand.

At the elevator he asked, "Do people ever change their mind?"

The woman hit the call button. "About what?"

He didn't answer.

The elevator doors opened.

In the mirrored wall inside, he saw the old body one last time. Work shirt under the jacket. Hair flattened from the headset band. Tired face. He looked wrecked. More than that, he looked like he had been trying for too long to keep something from showing.

By the time they reached the transfer floor, he was too worn out to keep asking questions.

Across the city, Jonathan Mercer stood in his office staring at an alert on a dashboard he should have shut off months ago.

CONTINUITY ESCALATION
Subject: Eli Mercer
Assisted residency support engaged

For a second he thought he had read it wrong.

Then he opened the trace.

The chain underneath was clean, fast, automated almost all the way through. Assisted continuation. Profile preservation. Residency stabilization. Jonathan read it once and felt his stomach turn. Not fake, exactly. Worse than that. Eli had wanted something. They'd taken that and fed it through a system until it came out looking official.

He entered an override.

ACCESS DENIED
CONFLICT OF INTEREST FLAGGED

He tried a second route with compliance credentials.

REQUEST ESCALATED
STANDBY

Jonathan grabbed his coat.

Before he reached the door, an internal message flashed across his comm from a blocked sender.

He's not being taken.
He initiated continuity.

Jonathan stared at the two lines until anger got through the shock.

He called Eli.

No answer.

He called again while walking. By the time he stepped into the hallway, two security officers were already there by the elevator.

"I'm Director of Compliance," he said.

"That's why we need you to remain available," one of them replied.

"Move."

"Sir."

Jonathan looked from one to the other and saw it at once. Whatever argument remained here was for appearances. The decision had already moved past him.

He stopped calling. There was no point doing it with them standing there.

Instead he stood in the polished corridor outside his own office and thought about every clean word on the dashboard, every way a system could take a frightened yes and sand it down until it passed for process.

When consciousness came back, it did not hit her all at once.

She noticed the sheet first.

Not silk. Not anything theatrical. Just fabric that wasn't pulling wrong anywhere. Then the air on her skin. Then the way the mattress held her without the usual little adjustments. She opened her eyes and stayed still.

A room came into focus around her. Soft light. Pale horizon through a broad window. No hallway smell. No elevator hum. No fluorescent strip buzzing overhead.

She took a breath.

Nothing caught.

That was the first thing that really registered. Not a thought. Just the body itself. Chest opening cleanly. Shoulders not wound up around her neck. Jaw not already set against the day.

Memory came back in scattered pieces.

The shelter glass.
Registered baseline.
The hallway.
"If you come with us, the profile is preserved."

She sat up slowly.

A display opened along the wall.

Residency confirmed.
Continuity stable.
Adaptive environment synchronized.

The words should have felt like a lock.

Instead she was mostly aware of her own body. The weight of it. The balance. The fact that sitting up hadn't required the usual quick correction, that almost invisible brace she had done for so long she barely noticed it outside.

She swung her legs over the side of the bed and stood.
